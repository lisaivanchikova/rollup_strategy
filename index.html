03 · The Replication Unit

The unit that scales is the playbook — not the engineer, the customer, or the dollar.

Each business model has a different replication unit, and each unit has a different bottleneck and ceiling. Stacking three units — playbook + customer-unit + acquired company — gives Integration Co three compounding speeds that reinforce each other.

The playbook in six components

The strategic question is which unit scales the operating company. The answer is the playbook — a pre-built vertical-specific package the team plugs into each engagement. The playbook is what makes a 5-day customizer beat a 30-day bespoke build. Six components:

01 · Vertical thesisWhy this vertical's SMEs deserve AI integration, what AI can actually move (labor cost, error rate, ticket volume).
02 · Baseline kitPre-built measurement infrastructure for L1 diagnostic — KPIs, data taps, comparison benchmarks.
03 · Reference architectureStack of agents / workflows / integrations validated in prior engagements — 70% reusable, 30% customized.
04 · Pricing templateL1 fee / L2 gainshare cap / L3 warrant terms calibrated for vertical-specific EBITDA and exit-multiple ranges.
05 · Operations runbook30/60/90 day deployment checklist, escalation paths, SLA monitoring.
06 · Acquisition due-diligence packPre-completed financial / tech-debt / customer-concentration audit format for L3 conversion candidates.

Leverage hierarchy: Engineer (linear) < Customer-unit (compounding) < Playbook (step-function) ≈ Acquired-Co (capital-bound). Integration Co stacks all three productive ones — playbook + customer-unit + acquired-Co — so three scaling speeds reinforce each other in parallel.

04 · The Vertical Entry

Every business runs the same 4 support functions — they are our universal vertical entry points.

The standard business-process map separates every company on earth into three layers: management process (CEO/strategy), core process (industry-specific value creation), and support processes (the back-office that runs identically across all industries). The support layer is short — four functions: HR, IT, Finance, Legal. Plumbing companies run them. Law firms run them. HomeCare agencies run them. One AI integration playbook for a support function plugs into 80%+ of all US SMEs regardless of industry.

This is the entry asymmetry. We sell one playbook per support function, deploy it through one vendor that already serves the SME's industry (HR agency, MSP, accounting firm), and earn warrants on all of them. The vendor becomes the funnel; the playbook becomes the IP.

06 · The IEC Hybrid

Combining models #6 + #7 + #8 into one offer — Integrate, Earn, Convert. Three gates, each optional.

IEC stitches the three SME-viable archetypes into a sequential offer. Each layer gates the next: L1 fee filters tire-kickers and funds delivery; L2 gainshare only triggers if savings are real; L3 warrants only convert if the customer crosses the L3 threshold. No incumbent runs all three together — and the three together are what makes the fund's acquisition pipeline a byproduct of the services arm.

Start inside the fund for speed; convert to stand-alone at $3M ARR — disclosure execution matters more than the structure choice itself.

The binding framework today is the pre-2023 baseline: Section 206 anti-fraud + general fiduciary duty (SEC v. Financing the L1 fee — how SMEs without $25K cash become paying customers

The SME budget problem is real: 5–50 FTE businesses do not have $25K of discretionary cash. We solve it by financing the L1 fee against the L2 cash flow we ourselves will collect — the same mechanic vendor financing has used in equipment, energy ESCO, and tax-recovery contingency for decades. Four paths, used in a blend that targets a ~7–9% blended cost of capital:

PathMechanicCostWhen to use
A · Self-financed30–40% of L1 clients pay upfront; that cash funds the deferred 60–70%0% — internalDefault. Anchor at 60% of L1 volume.
B · Revenue-based financingL2 gainshare contracts as receivables; Capchase / Pipe / Lighter Capital advance10–12% flat (~12–20% APR)~25% of volume — for SME clients with verifiable savings baseline.
C · Fund working-capital facilityBobroff fund extends WC line to Integration Co secured against L2 contracts8–10% (treasury + spread)~15% of volume. Requires LPA-level disclosure (Section 206 nuance).

Counter-intuitive selection mechanic: a $5K paid audit (credited 100% against L1 fee if client proceeds) converts at 25–45% vs. 10–20% for a free audit. The $5K fee filters tire-kickers and doubles conversion of the funnel — recovering its own cost in CAC reduction.

The same four-entity skeleton supports both models

Only the ownership line between Fund/HoldCo and Integration Co changes. Everything downstream — HoldCo holding the QSBS-eligible C-corp wrapper, OpCos paying for services at arm's-length, founder minority retention — is identical across both architectures. Model A wraps Integration Co as a wholly-owned subsidiary of the fund (Vista Consulting Group precedent, 30–60 day setup, but 206(3) friction on every warrant). Model B carves Integration Co out as an independent Delaware C-corp with the fund as sub-25% anchor (KKR Capstone precedent, 3–6 month setup, but ISO grants + outside capital + Eudia-style contingent capital unlocked).
