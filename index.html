01 · Executive Summary

Bobroff fills the only services-plus-acquisitions quadrant the $1.5B incumbents can't enter.

The category in one paragraph. The "AI roll-up" market crystallized in 2024-2026 around a single insight: mature US services SMEs (CPA firms, HOA managers, MSPs, home care, law firms) operate at 20-40% EBITDA margins that AI can double in 12 months. Three structural archetypes have formed: VC-backed Creation Studios (General Catalyst $1.5B, Thrive Holdings $1B+, Elad Gil $1.5B), Permanent-Capital Compounders (Constellation, Roper, ESW Capital), and Services-First Operators (Distyl $1.8B, Accenture, Bain & Co). The structural gap Bobroff fills at sub-$100M scale is unique: no incumbent combines AI integration services sold to third-party SMEs WITH acquisitions of SMEs in the same vertical, where the services arm feeds the acquisition pipeline with live operational telemetry. Capital structure per S16VC Fund 3 base case: $52M total equity through Series A across 5 verticals — $25.5M from S16 + $26.5M from pre-Series B co-investors. Additional acquisition firepower via vendor financing (30%) and debt (15%) brings total deployable to ~$95M.

02 · Business Model Framework

Every business model decomposes into four components — and the replication unit defines the ceiling.

ComponentDefinitionKey choices for Integration Co
1. Value Proposition What we actually sell. The customer-facing product or outcome. AI integration / ongoing AI ops / acquisition exit upside / data telemetry / capital access
2. Customer Who pays us. The buyer segment, deal size, sales cycle. SME owners ($1-10M revenue, 5-50 FTE) / PE acquirers / other integrators / lenders
3. Monetization mechanic How money flows. Pricing structure, payment cadence, margin profile. One-time fee / subscription / per-unit / share-of-savings / equity warrants / acquisition carry
4. Replication unit What scales. The lever you pull to turn small into big. Different in every model. Engineer / customer / vertical playbook / acquired company / partner node / data point

04 · Emerging Models 2024–2026

Outcome-based pricing wins, but only with a human-equivalent quality floor (Klarna lesson).

Per-resolution / per-actionSierra AI charges only when agent resolves customer issue autonomously; human escalations are free. Per-resolution $1-2.50. Hit $100M ARR in <2 years. Source: Sierra blog; Sacra
Outcome-as-subscription (Eudia model)Crete/Eudia sell legal/accounting as fixed monthly fee, not hourly — AI absorbs marginal labor cost. Customer pays for "complete outcomes," vendor captures all efficiency gain. Source: GC Eudia
Risk-sharing pilotsFree/below-cost first 60 days, then % of savings. Distyl's "produce first outcome together" go-to-market. Sales cycle 3-9 months vs 6-18 months for T&M.
Managed Intelligence Provider (MIP)MSP evolution: IT services + AI automation in single MRR bundle. 85-90% client retention reported. Source: GetThread
Failure mode: Klarna. AI agent replaced ~853 FTEs and saved $60M/yr. CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski publicly walked back the "no humans" stance in May 2025 — quality issues forced rehiring. Lesson: outcome-based models collapse without a quality floor. Every AI services contract needs an SLA tied to human-equivalent benchmark. Source: Bloomberg

05 · Replication Units

Stack three units — playbook + customer-unit + acquired company — to compound at three different speeds.

Per Nicolas's framing: "every business is a unit that replicates thousands of times. A restaurant chain replicates restaurants; a packaging company replicates sales channels × geographies. What is the replication unit of an Integration Company?" The answer depends on which business model you pick — and each unit has a different bottleneck and different ceiling.

07 · Strategic Insights

Seven non-consensus observations that together explain why IEC is achievable now.

1Services + Acquisitions in same vertical = unique flywheel nobody runs at SME scale. Services arm provides live P&L telemetry on which customers to acquire. Acquisitions provide proof points to sell services. Both reinforce. GC does (b) only at $1.5B. Distyl does (a) only at $1.8B. Sub-$100M is structurally open.
2AI-as-installer is throwaway revenue; AI-as-operator is durable. Most consultants charge for installation, then leave. Integration Co can charge for ongoing operation forever, because AI requires tuning, data, model updates. The "ongoing" layer (Gainshare MRR + Per-unit fee) is what compounds — not the initial integration fee.
3SMEs have no budget for consulting fees but have budget for cost reduction. Share-of-savings is mathematically dominant in SME segment because it (a) aligns risk, (b) removes upfront capex objection, (c) allows uncapped upside for vendor. Limit: SMEs lack baseline measurement — this is why incumbents skip SME. Solution: $2.5-5K productized diagnostic that builds measurement infrastructure as Layer 1.
4The replication unit determines fund-vs-operating-company economics. If unit = customer → SaaS fund (Vista pattern). If unit = engineer → services fund (Accenture pattern). If unit = acquired company → roll-up fund (S16 pattern). If unit = partner → marketplace fund (uncommon). These have fundamentally different IRR structures. Bobroff should stack three units — playbook + customer-unit + acquired-Co — so they compound at different speeds simultaneously.
5The "Agency" toggle in S16 model is dramatically undervalued. The model assigns 50% probability — that's a placeholder. Reality: Agency at scale = Constellation Software economics on tiny capital base. In Upside case, Agency layer EV = $5.6B on ~$14.5M capital — 390x. Even at half probability, it's a top-quartile compounder. Any product-led strategy that builds a SaaS overlay BEFORE deploying acquisition capital captures this upside; pure roll-up strategies don't.
6Vendor financing for AI installation is the hidden bottleneck. SMEs can't pay $50-200K upfront for AI installation. Three solutions, all with precedent: (a) financed installation as a loan repaid from savings, (b) pay-as-you-save with zero upfront, (c) equity warrants instead of cash (Pioneer Square Labs model — penny warrants for 2-7% of company per $50K services value).
7The "buy the customer" arbitrage. AI service customers who succeed become acquisition targets with unique advantages: we already know their financials (we automate them); we know their AI uplift (we provided it); we have switching cost (they depend on our tools); we can acquire at private multiple (4-5x EBITDA) and exit at public multiple (12-15x). This is exactly the IEC model — Integrate-Earn-Convert — and it works only if the Fund and Integration Co operate as legally separate entities with information-sharing arrangements that survive SEC Section 206 scrutiny.

08 · The IEC Hybrid Model

Integrate → Earn → Convert. Three gates, each optional, each priced for the SME wallet.

Synthesized from all four research streams. The IEC model combines patterns from Sierra, GainShare AI, Toast, Distyl, Pioneer Square Labs, and General Catalyst into a structure none of them runs in full:

09 · Legal Structure

Start inside the fund for speed; convert to stand-alone at $3M ARR — disclosure execution matters more than the structure choice itself.

For IEC to work without triggering SEC Section 206 conflict-of-interest, the structure must legally separate Fund / HoldCo / Integration Co / OpCos. Two viable Integration Co configurations exist; the choice is the single most consequential legal decision in the fund's lifetime.

Choice — Integration Co inside the Fund vs as a Stand-Alone Startup

Dimension Model A · Inside the Fund (wholly-owned subsidiary) Model B · Stand-Alone Startup (fund as anchor, sub-25%)
Closest precedent Vista Consulting Group ($107B AUM, 100+ FTE captive); General Catalyst HATCo (Summa Health $485M acquisition outside fund cycle); Bain Capital Portfolio Group (115+ FTE) KKR Capstone (10-Q verbatim: "not a subsidiary or affiliate of KKR, operates under consulting agreements, uses KKR name under license"). 25+ years, no SEC enforcement
Setup time + cost 30–60 days; $50–100K legal (single LPA + ERA filing) 3–6 months; $50–100K legal + separate C-corp formation $5–15K + board/operating agreements $10–25K
Section 206(3) exposure High when affiliate threshold (>25% control) crossed. Each warrant from portco needs documented arms-length pricing + annual valuation Materially weakened if fund stays sub-25% and no control indicia (no board majority, no shared employees). KKR Capstone precedent holds
Talent equity Operators are fund partners; no separate ISO grants. Top operators prefer equity in the operating co specifically ISO grants enabled; critical at $260K+ base + $50K equity for Vertical Lead per 2026 AI-startup benchmarks (Carta, Sentiro Partners)
Outside capital Closed. Cannot take SaaS-strategic checks or growth equity without restructuring Open. Eudia-style contingent capital available ($30M upfront + $75M on milestone acquisitions GC approves)
Acquisition pipeline cleanliness Each Integration Co → portco transaction is intra-fund related-party Arms-length by default; no 206(3) trip wire on entry
Exit optionality Single — through fund liquidation; LP approval required Double exit: roll-up portfolio + Integration Co itself sold to SaaS strategic / growth equity at SaaS multiples (4–6× ARR)
LP narrative "We built the operating arm." Cleanest single story Two governance stories to maintain (fund LPA + Integration Co board); reporting/IR overhead doubles
Fund return capture 100% of Integration Co value (consolidated) Sub-25% of Integration Co terminal value flows to fund; rest to operators + outside investors
Recommendation — sequenced approach. Start Model A for first 12 months to compress setup time and preserve narrative simplicity. Convert to Model B at the trigger of $3M ARR or first L3 acquisition closed, whichever comes first. Pre-bake spin-out language into original LPA so conversion does not require renegotiation. This sequence mirrors General Catalyst's HATCo logic — operating assets cannot live indefinitely inside a 10-year fund cycle because the integration tail extends 10–15 years.

Why this matters — the 2024 Fifth Circuit window

Current regulatory regime is materially more permissive than 2023. On June 5, 2024, the Fifth Circuit vacated all five SEC Private Fund Adviser Rules of 2023 in National Association of Private Fund Managers v. SEC. The binding framework today is the pre-2023 baseline: Section 206 anti-fraud + general fiduciary duty (SEC v. Capital Gains Research Bureau 1963) + the 2019 SEC Fiduciary Duty Interpretation (IA-5248) + customary side-letter disclosure practice. Bobroff at $52M committed sits below the $150M Rule 203(m)-1 threshold — Exempt Reporting Adviser (ERA) status, partial Form ADV, Section 206 applies but full registration not required. Sources: Morgan Lewis · SEC announcement · 17 CFR § 275.203(m)-1

Pattern 2 — Payments-take is the silent ARR multiplier

Companies WITH embedded payments take rate consistently outperform per-seat-only on ARR per logo:
  • Toast: fintech revenue ≈ 4× subscription revenue per location ($51bps × $159B GPV = ~$810M payments)
  • Jobber: $13B GMV (2022) at 2.7% blended = potential $350M+ payments revenue exposure
  • Clio Payments + AppFolio Value+ + HoneyBook Finance + Mindbody payments — all follow same playbook
  • Karbon, Salesforce SMB, Practice Better (no payments take) — structurally lower ARR ceiling per logo
Implication for Integration Co: if we build an AI SaaS for SME, payments-take or equivalent transaction layer is a 2-4× ARR multiplier on the same install base.

Pattern 4 — Disclosure asymmetry creates strategic blind spots

Reliability gradient (most → least): Public SEC filers (Toast/ServiceTitan/Procore/AppFolio/HubSpot/Palantir/Constellation/Roper/Accenture) → Public partnership annual disclosures (Deloitte/BCG) → Founder interviews via Latka/Sacra (HoneyBook/Clio/Mendix/Jobber) → Aggregator estimates (Tracxn/Growjo — conflict heavily, e.g. QuantumBlack revenue ranges $57M-$980M across sources) → Private firms with zero disclosure (Vista at fund level, ESW, Mindbody since 2019, Housecall Pro, Cresta financials, Forethought).

For investor strategy: treat any "ARR" number not in an SEC filing or audited press release as a Latka-interview triangulation. 60%+ of publicly-cited private SaaS ARR figures fall in this bucket.

Pattern 5 — Where Bobroff IEC model fits

The IEC (Integrate-Earn-Convert) model proposed earlier maps to a specific unit hierarchy stack:
  • L1 Integrate ($25K diagnostic+build): Productized engagement → playbook-as-unit (Distyl/Sierra archetype, scaled down to SME)
  • L2 Earn (15-25% gainshare MRR, capped): Per-saved-dollar unit (GainShare AI archetype, no SME incumbent productizes this)
  • L3 Convert (warrants + ROFR at pre-AI multiple): Per-acquired-company unit (Crete/Current archetype at SME scale)
Triple-unit stacking means Bobroff captures three different scaling speeds simultaneously: services (per-engagement, fastest cycle), gainshare (per-customer-recurring, compounds with customer's own savings), and acquisitions (capital-bound, longest cycle but largest exit). No incumbent in the 44-player landscape stacks all three.
