PART I · STRATEGY01The Question

Among the games available to operators with US market, SMB-acquisition and AI-deployment capabilities, which maximises probability-weighted founder cash-out at exit?

The question is asked working backwards from founder cash-out at exit, the realised quantity against which game choice is graded. Cash-out decomposes into Multiple × Scale × Retained Ownership (Scale = EBITDA in PE roll-up framing, ARR in SaaS framing); expected cash-out further multiplies by Probability of Exit on Horizon. Software-economics games maximise the first; acquisition-plus-AI games maximise the product weighted by probability. The choice is a preference declaration, not a ranking exercise.

Why this framing matters

Founder ownership at exit varies by an order of magnitude (~8× at midpoints; 4–15× across full ranges) across archetypes. Bootstrapped software founders retain 40–60%; VC-backed vertical SaaS founders retain 15–25%; frontier AI founders retain 4–10%. Ranges reflect collective founding-team retention at exit; per-founder figures roughly halve in 2–3 founder teams. Ranking by absolute cash-out diverges sharply from ranking by enterprise value when dilution is included. The same operator playing two different games receives wildly different outcomes — the game choice is upstream of every other decision.

PART I · STRATEGY04Pure HoldCo Posture

AI is deployed across acquired portfolio OpCos and inside operator-partners during paid pre-acquisition pilots that lead to acquisition. AI is not sold as a standalone subscription product to customers without acquisition intent.

What we do

  • Acquire operating SMBs in fragmented service verticals at SMB multiples; install AI inside each OpCo to compress integration and raise margins.
  • Run paid pre-acquisition pilots (typically 60–90 days, $100–300K fees) with operator-partners as the Stage 2 of the M&A funnel. Pilot fee is credited against acquisition price; ends with acquire or walk away, gated by ≥1.5× productivity uplift on operator-partner workflows (see §13).
  • Hold the AI Engineering team as an internal department of the HoldCo, not a separate legal entity transacting with portfolio cos.
  • Sell the consolidated platform at platform multiples through PE-to-PE recap or strategic sale.

What we do not do

  • License AI products as a standalone subscription business or to operators outside the acquisition pipeline.
  • Maintain a standing services arm sold to N customers in the broad market.
  • Engage in recurring services contracts unbounded by acquisition intent.
  • Stage 1 posture. This applies to Vintage 1 of the closed-end fund. Stage 2 productisation (Data Network) triggers re-architecture per §03 (Path C) and §15.

Why this posture, not a hybrid services arm

The hybrid services arm is rejected on five mechanisms (multiple compression, Section 206 conflict, bargaining position erosion, engineering bandwidth split, LP disclosure burden) enumerated in §03 Path F and developed in full in the Pure HoldCo Decision Memo.

Full mechanism analysis in Pure HoldCo Decision Memo → download HTML.
Paid pre-acquisition pilots are not a services arm

The five mechanisms above target a standing services business with N customers and recurring subscription revenue. Paid pre-acquisition pilots are 1–2 operator-partners per vertical, time-bounded 60–90 days, single fee credited against acquisition price, ending with acquire or walk away. All five documented reference operators (Long Lake, GC Creation, Shield, Crete / Current, NMC) run paid pre-acquisition engagements. The distinction is duration, counterparty count, fee structure, purpose, and end state.

PART I · STRATEGY06Vertical Thesis

Vintage 1 enters support-process verticals — back-office functions found inside every SMB, structurally fragmented and AI-replaceable at the workflow level.

Support processes — accounting, IT, HR, legal back-office, marketing, translation, collections, telemarketing — are universal back-office functions present in every operating company. They are not the customer's core business, so AI deployment does not touch revenue-generating workflows. They are structurally fragmented: tens of thousands of SMB providers serve sub-$10M-EBITDA customer pools without consolidation. They have high labour-replaceability via AI agents trained on structured inputs and outputs. And a single AI playbook (e.g., accounting close, ticket triage, candidate screening) is reusable across thousands of sub-segments within the same vertical.

Scoring methodology

V×C×A×B×M is the scoring framework maintained at the rollup-economy 6-digit NAICS map (1,012 cells, updated continuously). Each NAICS cell is scored 1–10 on five factors:

  • V — Value (labour replaceability). Share of vertical's labour cost that AI agents can absorb at near-equivalent quality.
  • C — Capture (fragmentation). Inverse concentration: how many independent operators exist below $10M EBITDA, ready for roll-up.
  • A — Acceleration (AI adoption speed). Time from AI-playbook ship to measurable margin improvement in a typical OpCo.
  • B — Buyability (industry size proxy). Total vertical revenue available as a buyable pool.
  • M — Multiple-arbitrage spread. Standalone-SMB exit multiple vs consolidated-platform exit multiple gap.

Composite score S = V × C × A × B × M. Hell-Yes threshold ≥21,600 ≈ geometric mean ≥7.4 across the five factors at the 6-digit NAICS cell basis.

APPENDIX B

Appendix B · Horizontal AI Platform — Reserve Alternative Game

Horizontal AI is treated as a distinct game with different economics, team, and capital model. It is held as the reserve alternative if the chosen-game thesis fails validation. The following is the brief operating-model summary.

Brief operating model

  • Product: Foundation model, agent framework, or orchestration layer sold as agentic AI products to enterprises across many industries from one common code path.
  • Sub-types: Frontier model labs (Anthropic, OpenAI) and application-layer agent platforms (Sierra, Decagon, Glean, Harvey, Cresta).
  • Capital required to $100M ARR: $200–600M for application-layer; $5–20B+ for frontier model labs.
  • Founder ownership at exit: 4–15% application-layer; 0–8% frontier labs.
  • Exit multiples: 15–40× ARR application-layer; 50–100× ARR model labs in private rounds.
  • Pilot-to-production cliff: only 11–14% of enterprise AI pilots reach production at scale (IDC).
  • Failure mode: reverse acqui-hire — Adept ($400M raised → Amazon talent absorption); Inflection ($1.5B+ → Microsoft $650M); Character.AI (Google).
Warning embedded in this appendix

A roll-up operator that drifts into selling AI services to non-portfolio customers without product discipline converges to a low-multiple consulting category (1–3× EBITDA, see Archetype 09). The risk is gradual: pilot fees → recurring contracts → subscription → undercapitalised SaaS. Each step lowers the multiple and locks in operating overhead. The Pure HoldCo posture exists specifically to prevent this drift.
