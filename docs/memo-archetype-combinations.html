Archetype Combinations and Sequencing — Which Games to Play, in What Order, and Why

The team has selected the AI-Native Operating Holdco archetype as the primary game. Three other archetypes from the Game Selection Framework — Permanent Capital Compounder, Horizontal AI Platform, Data Network Compounder — remain unexamined as potential complements or alternatives. This memo evaluates every viable combination, scores each against five structural criteria, and recommends a sequenced position with explicit triggers for stage progression.

01The Question

Now that AI-Native Operating Holdco is the chosen base archetype, which of the three remaining archetypes are complementary, which are sequential stages of the same game, and which are mutually exclusive alternatives?

Bottom Line Up Front

The four archetypes are not four equal alternatives — they live on three different strategic axes. Permanent Capital is a capital structure choice that can be layered on Holdco; Data Network is a product layer that can be added once data is accumulated; Horizontal AI is a distribution model that creates conflict with Holdco at one extreme and complements it at the other. Of six viable paths, one is recommended for the founding vintage (Solo Holdco), one conditional starting at Stage 2 (Holdco + Data Network productisation), one deferred to Vintage 2+ (Holdco + Permanent Capital). The Horizontal AI dimension is decomposed into five flavors evaluated independently — two recommended as non-revenue brand/talent assets, three rejected.

What we are deciding

  • The current Operating Model v4.0 specifies Solo Holdco with fund-vintage capital — pure Path A.
  • Three open questions surfaced by Nicolas: (1) can Holdco and Data Network be sequential stages of one model? (2) is Horizontal AI + Data Network a different game we should consider? (3) is a mix of all three coherent or capability-destroying?
  • Lisa adds: (4) add Permanent Capital to the combinatorial space; (5) in Horizontal AI, do not pre-judge — consider variants such as standalone team-built tools or industry-benchmark products that do not compete with portfolio AI.

What we are not re-litigating

The choice of AI-Native Operating Holdco as the base archetype is taken as decided per the Game Selection Framework (June 12, 2026, score 100/125, highest in the universe of fourteen archetypes). The choice of pure-Holdco posture without a standing services arm is taken as decided per the Pure HoldCo Decision Memo (June 14, 2026, five documented failure mechanisms). This memo asks the orthogonal question: given those decisions, what else fits?

02Reframing — Three Axes, Not Four Equal Archetypes

The four archetypes appear coordinate but are not — they describe three distinct strategic dimensions, which is why their combinatorial space collapses from fifteen to six.

If the four archetypes are treated as mutually exclusive operating models, the combinatorial space is 2^4 − 1 = 15 possible combinations. This produces a memo that is unreadable and an analysis that is structurally wrong. The reduction comes from recognising that the four archetypes occupy three different categorical positions:

ArchetypeWhat it actually isCombination logic
AI-Native Operating Holdco Operating model — what we do day-to-day (acquire SMBs, install AI, run a multi-OpCo platform) This is the base layer. Every viable path begins with Holdco as the operating spine.
Permanent Capital Compounder Capital structure — how we raise and hold money (fund vintage vs evergreen) Layered onto Holdco. Berkshire is a Holdco — with permanent capital. Markel is a Holdco — with permanent capital plus insurance float. Long Lake is a Holdco — with fund-vintage capital. Same operating spine, different capital chassis.
Horizontal AI Platform Distribution model — how AI reaches markets (internal to portfolio vs external to non-portfolio customers) Compatible with Holdco only in specific flavors; one flavor (vertical-AI-sold-externally) is structurally incompatible and was rejected by the Pure HoldCo Decision Memo. Other flavors evaluated below.
Data Network Compounder Product layer — what we build on top of accumulated data (sell aggregated dataset access) Sequential stage on top of Holdco, not parallel game. Requires data accumulation that only Holdco can produce. Requires 15–30 OpCos in a single vertical before the dataset has critical mass.
Why this matters operationally

Treating these as four equal archetypes leads to false dichotomies ("should we be Holdco or Permanent Capital?"). The right question is "should our Holdco use fund-vintage or permanent capital structure, and at what stage?". Same for Data Network — not "Holdco or Data Network" but "at what point in the Holdco lifecycle does Data Network productisation become viable?".

Empirical evidence that the axes are independent

  • Berkshire (Holdco + Permanent Capital, no Data Network, no Horizontal AI) — $1T+ market cap, $171B float, 60+ subsidiaries; ran for 60 years on this configuration. Source: Berkshire 2024 Annual Report.
  • Markel (Holdco + Permanent Capital + insurance underwriting) — Markel Ventures >20 wholly-owned subsidiaries; insurance float; founded 1930, IPO 1986. Source: Markel Group 2024 Annual Report.
  • Verisk (Data Network on top of acquired insurance businesses) — $2.88B FY24 revenue, 54.7% adjusted EBITDA margin; began as insurance company, built data network through ~70 acquisitions over two decades. Source: Verisk 10-K FY2024.
  • Plaid (Pure Data Network, no Holdco, no Permanent Capital) — $546M ARR, fund-vintage VC capital. Source: Sacra Plaid profile.
  • Long Lake (Pure Holdco, fund-vintage capital, no Data Network, no Horizontal AI) — $1.28B raised, ~30 acquisitions. Source: PitchBook Long Lake profile, May 2026 Amex GBT take-private press materials.
  • Sierra (Pure Horizontal AI, no Holdco, fund-vintage capital) — $150M ARR, $15.8B valuation at Series E, no acquisitions. Source: CMSWire, CNBC May 2026.

Each operator occupies a different position in the 3D space (operating × capital × product layer). They do not compete for the same square — they instantiate different combinations.

Sources: Berkshire 2024 Annual Report; Markel Group 2024 Annual Report; Verisk 10-K FY2024; Sacra Plaid; PitchBook Long Lake; CMSWire on Sierra Series E.

Combinations not analysed and why

  • Solo Permanent Capital, Solo Data Network, Solo Horizontal AI — these are baseline alternatives without Holdco. Each requires capital and capability sets distant from ours.   • All four together (Holdco + Permanent + Data + Horizontal) — only Brookfield comes close (Holdco + Permanent + light Horizontal via fund-management products + Data through Wealth Solutions analytics). Brookfield runs ~7,000 employees and $1T+ AUM. Out of scope for a $50M fund.

04Evaluation Framework — Five Structural Questions

Each path is scored against the five questions that determine whether a configuration is operationally coherent, capital-feasible, and supported by reference-operator evidence.

QDimensionThe test
Q1 Capital required Does the configuration fit within $50M Vintage 1 fund capacity, or does it require capital we do not have? Pass = fits. Conditional = fits with milestones. Fail = capital mismatch >2×.
Q2 Team capability fit Does our team (M&A diligence + AI Engineering + integration ops) execute the configuration without hiring an entire additional discipline at scale?
Q3 Sequencing with Holdco Does this configuration enable Holdco, sit alongside it without resource contention, or compete with it for management bandwidth?
Q4 P&L architecture Does this produce a single consolidated P&L narrative for LPs, or does it fracture into multiple sub-entities that LPs must underwrite separately? Single P&L = clean LP story.
Q5 Reference operator evidence Are there documented reference operators executing this configuration at our scale today? Pass = multiple operators, >3 years of evidence. Conditional = 1–2 operators or precedent in adjacent shapes. Fail = no operator runs this — be the first only with conviction.

Verdict semantics

  • STRONG — clear pass on the dimension, no constraints.
  • CONDITIONAL — passes if specific preconditions hold (capital milestone, team build-out, sequencing trigger).
  • MARGINAL — meaningful risk; requires explicit mitigation; pursue only with conviction.
  • FAIL — does not meet the test at current scale; revisit if scale changes.

Path-level verdict is not the arithmetic mean of dimension scores. A FAIL on any one of Q1, Q2, or Q5 typically dominates and blocks the path; FAIL on Q3 or Q4 indicates the configuration is workable but suboptimal.

Framework derived from Pure HoldCo Decision Memo's five-mechanism analysis (June 14, 2026) extended to combinatorial-archetype scope.

06Path B · Holdco + Permanent Capital Migration

Vintage 1 runs as closed-end fund. After demonstrating multiple-arbitrage returns, Vintage 2+ migrates to evergreen capital structure — publicly listed Holdco, insurance float, or perpetual private vehicle. The Berkshire / Markel architecture, but staged.

PATH B · DEFERRED
Holdco + Permanent Capital migration starting Vintage 2 or later
Q1 · CapitalMARGINAL
Q2 · Team fitCONDITIONAL
Q3 · SequencingCONDITIONAL
Q4 · P&LSTRONG
Q5 · ReferenceSTRONG

Verdict: Defer to Vintage 2+, trigger on Vintage 1 success

Why this is structurally attractive

Permanent capital removes the fund-vintage exit clock. Berkshire compounded book value per share at ~18.3% CAGR over 60 years (1965–2024) because it never had to sell. The same Holdco operating engine becomes worth dramatically more when held indefinitely: the math of compounding 12% per year for 25 years versus exiting at year 7–12 differs by an order of magnitude in cumulative wealth, even at modest CAGRs. Markel Group is the documented mid-scale precedent — IPO 1986 at ~$130M market cap, today a multi-billion-dollar permanent-capital compounder with Markel Ventures running >20 wholly-owned operating businesses alongside specialty insurance.

Why it cannot be Vintage 1

  • Capital structure mismatch. $50M closed-end fund cannot itself become permanent capital — that requires either an IPO ($100M+ market cap floor with multi-year operating record), an insurance company acquisition ($150M+ starting reserves for credible underwriting), or a multi-year evergreen LP raise. The Permanent Capital Compounder deep dive notes Loews Corp started with the Tisch brothers' $125,000 in a Lakewood, NJ hotel — proof that starting capital can be modest, but the compounding ran over decades before becoming a permanent-capital vehicle. We do not have decades; we have one vintage.
  • LP base mismatch. Closed-end LPs subscribe to a 7–12 year vintage with explicit exit liquidity. Asking those LPs to convert to permanent equity mid-vintage is a structural conflict and typically requires GP repurchase, not a continuation vehicle.
  • Reference operators all started from operating businesses with float access. Berkshire was a struggling textile mill with embedded book value. Markel was a specialty insurer already underwriting before IPO. Loews acquired CNA Financial in 1974 for $1B+ when its core was already $300M+ market cap. None of them started as a fund and migrated — they started as an operating chassis.

Realistic migration path

  • Vintage 1 (Years 0–6): Run Path A as planned. Deploy $50M into first vertical; reach 4–7 OpCos; demonstrate AI uplift; complete first exits or interim recap at attractive multiples.
  • Vintage 2 (Years 6–10): Raise $200–500M with proven track record. Begin negotiating evergreen vehicle structure with LPs who explicitly want long-hold exposure. Markel's transition from insurer to Holdco took similar time.
  • Vintage 3+ (Years 10+): Migrate to publicly listed Holdco or perpetual vehicle. By this stage the operating record justifies a public market valuation; LP base can include long-duration capital.

07Path C · Holdco + Data Network as Stage 2 Productisation

After 15–30 OpCos in a single vertical accumulate proprietary cross-OpCo data, productise the dataset and sell access via API or dashboard to non-competing buyers. Verisk-style staged emergence from operating business into data network.

PATH C · CONDITIONAL STAGE 2
Holdco + Data Network productisation at Stage 2, conditional on reaching 15+ OpCos in a single vertical
Q1 · CapitalCONDITIONAL
Q2 · Team fitMARGINAL
Q3 · SequencingSTRONG
Q4 · P&LSTRONG
Q5 · ReferenceCONDITIONAL

Verdict: Conditional Stage 2 — explicit trigger required

The mechanic that makes this attractive

Data Network economics are the strongest in the four-archetype universe: gross margins 74–90%, EBITDA margins 50–55% in mature operators, NRR 125–160% on consumption pricing (Snowflake 131% FY24, MSCI ~82% gross margin, Verisk 54.7% adjusted EBITDA). The defining mechanic is the two compounding asymmetries — time on supply (integrations take years to build) and switching cost on demand (customers build models on top of the schema) — producing multi-decade defensibility when combined with regulatory or trust moats. If we can stand up a data position naturally from Holdco operations, we capture this economic engine without the $50–200M pure-SaaS data buildout cost.

Why Stage 2, not Stage 1

  • Critical mass requirement. Verisk's ISO data pool was built over decades of insurer contributions; Plaid required >5,000 bank integrations before the network effect dominated competitive dynamics. The Data Network deep dive identifies integration cardinality as the unit of replication — Plaid 12,000+ institutions, Datavant 70,000+ partners, CoStar ~6M properties physically tracked. Our cross-OpCo dataset reaches meaningful scale only at 15–30 OpCos per vertical, which is Stage 2 territory.
  • Selling externally requires a buyer set distinct from portfolio competitors. Verisk sells ISO ratemaking data to insurers (its data contributors), but in a regulated context where state insurance commissioners require Verisk filings for rate approvals. CoStar sells CRE data to investors, brokers, lenders — not to other CRE operators that compete with itself. For Path C to work, the buyer set must be researchers, regulators, capital allocators, or adjacent industries — never the operators of our portfolio's competitive set.
  • Engineering bandwidth. Data Network requires a separate engineering function from AI uplift work — data pipeline, schema, normalisation, regulatory positioning, API surface. Vintage 1 cannot fund both teams at once; this becomes affordable only when Holdco operating cash flow plus Vintage 2 capital covers both.

Why this is fundamentally different from a standing services arm

The Pure HoldCo Decision Memo rejected a standing services arm via five mechanisms (multiple compression, Section 206 conflict, bargaining position erosion, engineering bandwidth split, LP disclosure burden). Data Network productisation behaves differently on three of those:

  • Multiple compression — does not apply. A data product is valued independently of operating businesses; investors do not blend a 20× ARR data multiple into a 12× EBITDA Holdco multiple, they value the data subsidiary separately. The blended multiple goes up when a high-multiple SaaS asset sits inside a Holdco — see Markel Ventures versus parent Markel Group.
  • Section 206 — substantially weakened. Selling aggregated benchmark data uniformly to a broad buyer set (not bespoke services to individual portfolio competitors) is not a related-party service arrangement. The Bloomberg / Verisk / MSCI precedents do not face routine Section 206 friction because data products at scale do not concentrate counterparty risk in any single related party.
  • Bargaining position erosion — does not apply. Data buyers (researchers, regulators, ETF issuers) are not competitors of our portfolio operators. Selling research-grade aggregated data to a state insurance commissioner does not erode our HomeCare OpCos' market position with patients.
  • Engineering bandwidth split — applies, must be staged. Same mechanism applies; mitigated by deferring to Stage 2 when Holdco cash flow supports parallel team build.
  • LP disclosure burden — applies but modest. A consolidated data subsidiary under Holdco is one entity, not a separate operating company. LP narrative is "we built a data product on top of our operating data" — coherent and well-received.

Trigger for activation

  • Holdco operates 15+ OpCos in a single vertical with consistent data capture.
  • Cross-OpCo dataset has documented coverage / freshness / linkage advantage over public benchmarks.
  • External buyer interest (informal interview signal from researchers, regulators, or insurance carriers — for the HomeCare vertical, plausibly CMS, MedPAC, or insurance carriers underwriting home-care liability).
  • Holdco cash flow plus Vintage 2 raise can fund an additional 5–10 person data-engineering team without competing with vertical roll-up bandwidth.
Sources: Data Network Compounder deep dive (Snowflake 10-K FY2024, Verisk 10-K FY2024, MSCI 10-K FY2024, Sacra Plaid profile); Pure HoldCo Decision Memo (services arm rejection mechanisms).

Q3 · SequencingCONDITIONAL

Verdict: Defer to Vintage 2+ — emerges naturally if B and C both succeed

Why this is the destination, not the starting point

Markel Group is the documented Path-D operator: permanent capital from insurance underwriting, >20 wholly-owned operating subsidiaries in Markel Ventures, data and analytics products in insurance, and explicit reframing in 2024 from book-value-per-share to intrinsic-value-per-share precisely because the non-insurance OpCos are not marked to fair value in book-value reporting. This is the configuration that compounds across decades — Markel founded 1930, IPO 1986, still expanding the platform. Verisk arrived at this position by spinning out of insurance company ISO over decades. Path D is not pursued — it emerges if Paths B and C both succeed in their respective stages.

Why it cannot be started early

  • Triple-team capability requirement. Holdco operations team + permanent-capital fundraising and IR + data product engineering. Three distinct functions that compete for management bandwidth.
  • Capital base requirement. Permanent capital needs decades of compounding or insurance float (Berkshire $171B float at year-end 2024) or an IPO at scale. Combining with data buildout adds an additional $50–200M of pure SaaS capital or equivalent acquisition-led data buildout.
  • Reference operators are decades old. Markel 96 years. Verisk's data lineage goes back to 1971 (ISO predecessor). Berkshire 60+ years. Path D rewards founders willing to commit decadal timelines — it does not reward acceleration.

What Path D looks like at Vintage 4–5 if everything works

A publicly listed Holdco with $1–3B market cap, 80–150 OpCos across 5–7 verticals, insurance float or evergreen capital base, AI Engineering arm of 30–60 engineers across operations and a data product subsidiary throwing off 25–40% margin on subscription revenue. This is the upper bound of what our team can be in 15–25 years. It is unrealistic at Vintage 1 and unproductive to optimise for now beyond keeping options open.

09Path E · Holdco + Horizontal AI Platform — Five Flavors Evaluated Separately

Horizontal AI Platform is not one option — it is a category with five flavors of distinct economics. Two flavors are recommended as non-revenue brand or talent assets, three are rejected on capital, conflict, or capability grounds.

The Pure HoldCo Decision Memo rejected a single flavor of Horizontal AI — selling our vertical-specific AI to non-portfolio operators in the same verticals. That rejection's five mechanisms (multiple compression, Section 206 conflict, bargaining position erosion, engineering bandwidth split, LP disclosure burden) are specific to that flavor and do not transfer wholesale to all variants. Each flavor must be evaluated against the mechanisms that actually apply.

E-1 · Vertical AI sold externally to non-portfolio operators
REJECT
Selling our HomeCare AI to other HomeCare operators outside the acquisition pipeline. Q1 PASS (low marginal capital), Q2 CONDITIONAL, Q3 FAIL (Sec 206 conflict, bargaining erosion), Q4 FAIL (separate P&L, LP disclosure), Q5 FAIL (no Holdco reference operator does this — Long Lake, Shield, Current, Crete all keep AI internal). Already rejected June 14 services memo. Inclusion here for completeness.
E-2 · Internal M&A operating tool, productised externally
CONDITIONAL — natural exhaust only
Build sourcing / diligence / integration tools for our own M&A pipeline (we will build them regardless), then license to other roll-up operators. Q1 STRONG ($200–500K incremental for productisation). Q2 CONDITIONAL (M&A team already builds these for internal use; productisation adds 1–2 engineers). Q3 CONDITIONAL (parallel to Holdco; small bandwidth cost). Q4 CONDITIONAL (small separate revenue line; absorbs into Holdco P&L as SaaS subsidiary). Q5 CONDITIONAL (PitchBook is the data exemplar; Affinity began as a VC tool; documented pattern but small absolute outcomes — Affinity 2024 revenue undisclosed, founded 2014, Series C $80M valuation 2021 per Bloomberg). Verdict: pursue only if the M&A tool is so good that buyers ask for it unsolicited.
E-3 · Industry benchmark / research / content product
CONDITIONAL — brand and deal-flow asset
Build a research tool, industry benchmark, content platform — analogous to a16z's content empire (a16z.com, podcasts, newsletter, Future), USV's blog (Fred Wilson 20+ years daily), YC's Startup School and free library, Bessemer's State of the Cloud report. Q1 STRONG ($500K–$2M annual content / engineering). Q2 MARGINAL (requires content / research / editorial talent distinct from M&A and AI Engineering). Q3 STRONG (parallel to Holdco; brand and deal-flow accelerant). Q4 STRONG (zero or minimal revenue; cost line absorbed into Holdco overhead as marketing / brand). Q5 STRONG (a16z, YC, USV, Bessemer all run this configuration at varied scales). Important caveat: none of these are directly monetised as standalone businesses. a16z content is brand and deal flow, not revenue. YC's free resources are funnel for the accelerator. Pursue only if scoped as a brand asset, not a P&L line.
E-4 · Foundation model / horizontal AI SaaS at enterprise scale
REJECT — capital mismatch
Build a Sierra / Decagon / Glean-style enterprise AI platform. Q1 FAIL — Horizontal AI deep dive documents $200–600M minimum capital to reach $100M ARR for application-layer platforms; Sierra raised ~$285M pre-Series E; Harvey raised >$1B total; Decagon $481M to $4.5B; frontier-model labs $5–20B+. Our $50M fund is below the floor. Q2 FAIL — requires 50–200 PhD-level ML researchers (frontier) or 30+ ML engineers plus enterprise CRO at Series B (application). Capability set incompatible with M&A and integration-ops mix. Q3 FAIL — would consume the entire fund and team, abandoning Holdco. Q4 N/A. Q5 N/A. Reject without further analysis.
E-5 · Open-source tool released by AI Engineering arm
CONDITIONAL — talent and brand asset
Release engineering tools or evals built for internal use as open source. Q1 STRONG (zero marginal capital). Q2 STRONG (same engineering team). Q3 STRONG (parallel to Holdco). Q4 STRONG (no revenue, no separate P&L). Q5 STRONG (Anthropic released Claude Code SDK and MCP standard; OpenAI released Whisper, Triton; Meta released PyTorch and Llama; Databricks acquired MosaicML in part for OSS reputation; common pattern in AI engineering). Reasons to pursue: talent recruiting (engineers want to be seen contributing to OSS), brand signalling to LPs that the AI Engineering arm is serious, free engineering review from external community. Reasons not to: maintenance burden, intellectual property exposure on proprietary playbook details. Pursue narrowly and selectively, not as strategic priority.

Aggregate verdict on Path E

Two flavors (E-3 industry research / content, E-5 OSS) are recommended as non-revenue assets that serve brand, deal flow, and talent. Two flavors (E-1 vertical AI sold externally, E-4 horizontal SaaS at enterprise scale) are rejected on grounds already documented. One flavor (E-2 internal tool productised) is conditional on natural opportunity only — do not engineer the product to be sold.

Where these intersect with our standing position

The team already does versions of E-3 implicitly — internal frameworks (Game Selection Framework, V×C×A×B×M scoring map at rollup-economy.com) are public-facing artefacts that signal seriousness. Continuing this with explicit framing as "the Holdco's research arm" requires no new operational commitment. Lusin × Glocap-style paid pilots that produce reusable methodology can plausibly feed into E-3 as case studies.

Why this is rejected at a glance

The reverse acqui-hire is the modal failure exit (Adept $400M raised → Amazon talent absorption; Inflection $1.5B+ → Microsoft $650M; Character.AI → Google).

When this could be revisited

Only if the founding team materially changes (acquisition of a senior enterprise CRO with $50M+ in enterprise pipeline already pre-committed) and an LP base materially distinct from current commitments emerges willing to underwrite frontier-AI capital intensity. 12Final Positioning — Recommended Sequence Across Vintages

Path A operates Vintage 1. Paths E-3 and E-5 run in parallel as brand / talent assets at zero strategic cost. Path C activates at Stage 2 (Year 4–6) if the data trigger fires. Paths B and D defer to Vintage 2+, emerging from operating success rather than from forward commitment.

Stage 1 — Vintage 1, Years 0–4

  • Operating spine: Path A as specified in Operating Model v4.0. First vertical (Hell-Yes per V×C×A×B×M scoring map) launches Q3 2026; 4–7 OpCos targeted; AI Engineering arm fully internal; paid pre-acquisition pilots covered.
  • Non-revenue assets in parallel: Path E-3 (industry benchmark, framework content, research output — extending what is already at rollup-economy.com and the Game Selection Framework). Path E-5 narrowly scoped (release internally-built evals or playbook tools as OSS when they have no IP-sensitive content). Neither requires significant incremental capital or team.
  • Explicitly not pursued at this stage: Paths B, C, D defer. Path E-1, E-4, F rejected. Path E-2 reserved for opportunistic activation only.

Stage 2 — Vintage 1 mid-cycle, Years 4–6

  • Path C activates conditionally if the data trigger fires: 15+ OpCos in a single vertical, documented cross-OpCo data advantage, external buyer interest (researchers, regulators, adjacent industries), and Holdco operating cash flow plus Vintage 2 raise capacity for parallel 5–10 person data team. If trigger fires, stand up the data subsidiary inside Holdco. If trigger does not fire, continue Stage 1 operating model into Vintage 2.
  • Vintage 2 raise begins. $200–500M target. Track record from Vintage 1 plus AI Engineering arm credibility plus (if applicable) early Path C signals.

Stage 3 — Vintage 2+, Years 6–12+

  • Path B activates conditionally as Vintage 2 structure: if LP base wants long-duration exposure and operating record supports it, negotiate evergreen vehicle / public listing / insurance float access. Markel-Berkshire architecture begins to emerge.
  • Path D emerges naturally if both B and C succeeded — the publicly-listed Holdco with operating subsidiaries plus productised data layer plus permanent capital base. This is not engineered; it is the inheritance of having played A → C → B in sequence successfully.

What this means for outside positioning

External narrative for Vintage 1: "AI-Native Operating Holdco — fund-vintage capital, single chosen vertical, paid pre-acquisition pilots feeding acquisition pipeline, no external AI sales. Research arm and OSS contributions support brand and talent." This is honest, defensible, and matches every reference operator's posture at our scale (Long Lake, Shield, Current at comparable early-stage). It does not promise future stages — Stage 2 and beyond are options purchased by Vintage 1 execution, not commitments.

What changes from current Operating Model v4.0

Nothing about the Vintage 1 operating plan changes. The contribution of this memo is to label the existing baseline as Path A, identify Paths E-3 and E-5 as zero-cost parallel activities consistent with what the team already does, and explicitly defer Paths B, C, D as Stage 2+ options with named triggers rather than open questions. 13Decision Tree — Triggers for Stage Progression

Three explicit triggers govern when to move from Stage 1 to Stage 2, and Stage 2 to Stage 3. Each trigger names the operational signal, the financial test, and the structural change required.

Vintage 1 Stage 1 — Path A in motion │ ├── Vintage 1 Stage 2 trigger (Year 4–6) │ ├── Operational: 15+ OpCos in primary vertical │ ├── Data signal: documented cross-OpCo data advantage over public benchmarks │ ├── Buyer signal: ≥2 informal interview commitments from non-competing buyers │ ├── Capital signal: Vintage 2 raise process opens at $200M+ target │ └── If all four → activate Path C; else continue Stage 1 into Vintage 2 │ ├── Vintage 2 Stage 3 trigger (Year 6–10) │ ├── Track record: Vintage 1 exits delivered ≥2.5× MOIC blended │ ├── LP signal: ≥1 anchor LP commits $50M+ to evergreen vehicle │ ├── Structure: legal counsel confirms permanent-capital migration viable without breaking Vintage 1 LP agreements │ └── If all three → activate Path B; else continue closed-end vintage structure │ └── Vintage 3+ Path D synthesis (Year 10+) ├── Both B and C are operational and producing ├── Holdco is publicly listed or has equivalent permanent capital base ├── Data subsidiary throws off ≥$30M ARR with ≥40% EBITDA margin └── Markel-Verisk-style platform; multi-decade compounding posture confirmed

What blocks each trigger

  • Path C blocked if: data does not accumulate (OpCos use different systems), buyer set turns out to be portfolio competitors only (Section 206 concern returns), or engineering bandwidth cannot be added without compromising vertical roll-up.
  • Path B blocked if: Vintage 1 returns are below 2× MOIC (LPs decline permanent-capital extension), legal structure conflict with existing LP agreements, or team does not have public-market-CEO bench depth.
  • Path D blocked if: either B or C remains incomplete, or if scaling adds operational complexity faster than data and capital advantages accrue. Path D is not a target — it is a possible inheritance.

What the team commits to today

  • Path A — committed. Operating Model v4.0 as the active plan.
  • Path E-3 — committed at zero incremental cost. Continue framework / research / benchmark content. Position as Holdco research arm.
  • Path E-5 — committed selectively. Release OSS evals and tools as they reach reusable state, with IP review.
  • Path C — committed to monitoring. Establish data capture standards across OpCos from Vintage 1 day one, so that if the trigger fires at Year 4–6 the dataset is ready, not greenfield.
  • Paths B, D — committed to optionality. Vintage 1 LP agreements structured to allow Vintage 2 conversion to evergreen if conditions met.
  • Paths E-1, E-2, E-4, F — not committed. Reject or hold for opportunistic activation only.
