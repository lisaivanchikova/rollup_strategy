AI-Native Operating Holdco

A holding company acquires operating SMBs in fragmented service verticals — homecare, MSPs, accounting, P&C insurance, property management, RCM — and installs AI inside each company to lift margins or productivity. Operating companies remain decentralised; the holdco supplies capital, engineering, integration playbook, and shared functions. Exit via recapitalisation or strategic sale.

01Executive Summary

The archetype runs a multiple arbitrage between fragmented SMB acquisition multiples and scaled platform exit multiples; AI is the value-creation lever inside each acquired company.

How this archetype is defined in our framework

A holding company acquires operating SMBs in fragmented service verticals — homecare agencies, MSPs, accounting firms, P&C insurance brokerages, property managers, RCM firms, dental practices — and installs AI inside each acquired business to raise margins, productivity, or both. Operating companies remain decentralised; the holdco supplies capital, AI engineering, an integration playbook, and shared functions (procurement, finance, HR). The defining economic mechanism is multiple arbitrage between two markets: the SMB market where the target trades at 3–6× EBITDA before AI uplift, and the scaled platform market where the same business, post-uplift and aggregated with peers, trades at 8–14× EBITDA. Exit is recapitalisation by a next-tier sponsor, strategic sale of the holdco, or sale of individual verticals. Of the fourteen archetypes in the universe, this is the only one that structurally engages all three of US-market, SMB-acquisition, and AI-deployment capabilities at full strength. Quantitatively: exit multiples 8–14× EBITDA post-uplift, capital required $200–500M sponsor fund + debt, founder economics ~20–35% blended (sponsor equity plus GP carry), scalability ceiling $1–5B platform EV, time horizon 7–12 years per fund vintage. The combined enabler-fit score is 100 out of 125 — the highest in the universe.

Bottom Line Up Front

The strategy buys $1M–$20M EBITDA service businesses at 4–8× EBITDA in fragmented verticals, installs AI to lift margins 200–500 bps and growth, and exits the scaled platform at 8–14× EBITDA. The arbitrage is highest in insurance brokerage (sub-$5M EBITDA 7.5–12× → platform 16–18×) and MSPs (sub-$1M 4–8× → platform 11–14×). Long Lake, General Catalyst's Creation portfolio, Shield Technology Partners, and Current (formerly Crete) are the most-documented operators; Smarter Technologies / NMC is the most-documented execution failure.

02The Logic — Pyramid Decomposition

Value is created at three layers: operational uplift inside each OpCo via AI, scale advantages across the platform, and multiple expansion at exit.

AI-Native Operating Holdco — Multiple Arbitrage Thesis │ ├── Apex · Exit Multiple Expansion (the prize) │ ├── SMB acquisition multiples: 4–8× EBITDA │ ├── Platform exit multiples: 10–16× EBITDA post-AI uplift │ └── Arbitrage requires verifiable EBITDA uplift at diligence │ ├── Middle · Replication Engine (the playbook) │ ├── Vertical-specific AI integration playbook (codified) │ ├── Central engineering team (5–15 ML + 2–5 ops per vertical) │ ├── Acquisition cadence: 1 per quarter to 1 per month │ └── Deployment time per OpCo: months to days as playbook matures │ └── Base · OpCo Operational Uplift (the value-creation lever) ├── 25–30% per-FTE productivity (Long Lake HOA) ├── 31% time savings on labor-intensive tasks (Current tax prep) ├── 200–500 bps EBITDA margin uplift on labour-cost-heavy verticals └── Cultural acceptance and change management are binding constraints
Reading the pyramid

The thesis collapses if any layer fails. No verifiable uplift means no multiple expansion. No replication playbook means each OpCo is treated as a one-off and fund-level returns dilute toward standard mid-market PE (2× MOIC). No multiple expansion at exit means the strategy degenerates into a buy-and-hold operator vehicle without the platform premium.

03Business Model

The holdco buys established cash-flowing SMBs, installs AI inside each, and runs the consolidated entity as a multi-OpCo platform.

What the holdco buys

  • Vertical: HOA management, MSPs/IT services, accounting/CPA firms, P&C insurance brokerages, home health/home care, property management, revenue cycle management, professional consulting, dental/medical practices.
  • Size: $1M–$20M EBITDA for individual bolt-ons; $5M–$50M EBITDA for platform companies. Thoreau-scale platforms assemble already-large assets ($1B+ each).
  • Owner profile: 55–70 years old, often founder/operator, no internal succession. ~40% of US small businesses are Boomer-owned; 60%+ lack a written succession plan. ~$10T in private business assets coming to market by 2030 (Silver Tsunami).
  • Operating profile: 8–35% EBITDA margins pre-AI; recurring or quasi-recurring revenue; local market position; high labour intensity relative to revenue — the lever AI can move.

What the holdco produces

Two intertwined outputs: AI-augmented OpCos retaining their legacy customer relationships and licensing footprint, but with AI agents embedded in tax prep, HOA presentation drafting, MSP ticket triage, RCM coding, broker placement; and scaled platforms with multiple acquired OpCos under one holdco, sharing AI infrastructure, brand architecture, and back-office services.

Margin uplift evidence

  • Long Lake HOA: 25–30% per-FTE productivity gains; sales pipeline 10× larger via AI lead generation (General Catalyst "The Future of Services" essay). AI deployment time compressed from over a year to days as playbook matures.
  • Current (formerly Crete) Tax AI: 31% average time savings on tax prep; 98% accuracy across 7,000 returns in 2025 season; one accountant went from 180 hours to 15. OpenAI / Thrive Tax AI accuracy improvement from 25% to 97% in weeks via reinforcement on human corrections.
  • Shield Tech: 9 MSP partners collectively serve >1,500 customers; surpassed $100M ARR in 2025.
  • GC Creation portfolio: claims doubling of EBITDA margins within 12 months; some companies hitting $100M EBITDA in <2 years.

Caveat: these are operator self-reports; no independent diligence is yet public. MIT 2025 study found 95% of corporate AI projects fail to deliver measurable returns; 42% of companies abandoned most AI initiatives in 2025; Gartner reports 85% of AI/ML projects fail to deliver expected value. The contrast between operator claims and aggregate adoption data is the central diligence question for this archetype.

Three representative operators

  • Long Lake Management Holdings. Founded 2023. Raised $1.28B from General Catalyst, Thrive Capital, Alpha Wave Global, Lightscape Partners. ~18–30 acquisitions across HOA, residential services, business services, infrastructure services. May 2026: announced $6.3B acquisition of NYSE-listed Amex GBT at $9.50/share (60% premium), with GC and Alpha Wave equity-supporting the take-private. Targets 3–5 year hold to outperform S&P 500 via AI deployment across Amex GBT's marketplace.
  • GC Creation (General Catalyst Creation Strategy). $1.5B dedicated vehicle inside GC's $8B Fund XII raise. Led by MD Marc Bhargava. Targets the $16T global services economy with AI-native NewCo companies that acquire legacy service providers in their vertical. Long Lake is the flagship Creation portfolio company.
  • Shield Technology Partners. Launched June 2025 by Thrive Holdings + ZBS Partners. $100M initial + $100M Feb 2026 = $200M committed. Nine MSP partners as of Feb 2026; >1,500 customers; $100M+ ARR. Acquires 60–90% stakes; sellers retain leadership and local brand. December 2025: OpenAI took equity in Thrive Holdings; Shield gains direct OpenAI talent and model access.
Sources: PitchBook Long Lake profile; PYMNTS, Travel Weekly on Amex GBT take-private; General Catalyst "The Future of Services" essay; Sourcery on GC Creation; SiliconANGLE, BusinessWire on Shield; Current/Crete rebrand press June 2026.
04The Replication Unit

The scaling unit is the vertical-specific deployment playbook; each new OpCo is a marginal application of the playbook, each new vertical is a new playbook to write.

The atomic unit

Two candidates: per-acquisition at the OpCo level (each acquired SMB is the unit of integration and AI deployment), and per-AI-playbook deployment cycle within a single vertical (Long Lake's compression from over a year to days reflects optimisation of the cycle itself, not just the count). The true scaling unit is the vertical-specific deployment playbook — the codified set of AI agents, integrations, change-management materials, and pricing/comp models for a single vertical.

Cost to deploy one more OpCo (post first-three-in-vertical)

  • Transaction fee: ~2% of EV to advisors / bankers / legal; structuring + diligence $300K–$1.5M per transaction.
  • Integration team time: 5–15 engineers + ops per vertical playbook; supported on multiple OpCos in parallel.
  • AI installation work: $500K–$3M upfront engineering per playbook; near-zero marginal cost per OpCo once playbook exists.
  • Total marginal cost per new OpCo: $500K–$1.5M, small relative to EBITDA acquired.

Portable across acquisitions

  • AI integration playbook (prompts, agents, eval suites, training data)
  • Shared engineering team and central data platform
  • Procurement leverage (software, insurance, healthcare benefits)
  • Fund capital and acquisition financing
  • Brand for sourcing (sellers prefer a known acquirer)
  • LP relationships and debt market access

Stays local

  • Operating team and front-line staff (retained, not replaced)
  • Customer relationships, contracts, SLAs
  • Local regulatory licensing (CPA, insurance, Medicare certification, state homecare licensure)
  • Customer-facing brand (Shield, Current, Long Lake retain local sub-brands)
  • Real estate, vendor contracts, sometimes local IT systems during integration

Diseconomies of scale

  • Coordination overhead exceeds AI uplift when central team must support >15–20 OpCos across multiple verticals. Long Lake stayed primarily in HOA + adjacent residential/services for its first ~18 acquisitions plausibly because of this constraint.
  • Cultural fragmentation. AI deployment that worked in OpCo #1 hits resistance in OpCo #10 with different culture; 75% of employees lack confidence using AI; 65% fear AI will eliminate their roles (industry surveys 2025).
  • Multi-vertical playbook dilution. GC Creation operates across legal, IT, and other services; the deeper question is whether one engineering team can hold three playbooks. Thoreau's choice to consolidate five already-large healthcare-tech assets ($30B aggregate) is a different solution.
Sources: General Catalyst on Long Lake deployment compression; Bloomberg, Axios Pro on Thoreau structure; MIT 2025 enterprise AI failure study; CIO Magazine on adoption resistance.

Arbitrage is highest in insurance brokerage (most consolidated comp set) and MSPs (most active PE buyer interest, with multiples up 0.25–0.50× in 2025 per IBBA). Lowest in home care, where Medicare reimbursement caps create natural compression.

Illustrative unit economics ($5M EBITDA MSP target)

  • Acquisition price: $5M × 6× = $30M.
  • Debt at 60% of EV (HoldCo + OpCo) = $18M; equity = $12M.
  • AI deployment cost: $1M one-time.
  • 18 months later, EBITDA = $5M + 400 bps margin uplift on $25M revenue = $5M + $1M = $6M (20% EBITDA growth).
  • Exit at platform multiple of 11× = $66M EV. After debt repayment $18M, equity = $48M.
  • Equity return: 4× MOIC on 4-year hold ≈ 41% IRR.

This is the operator's pitch. Contra is multiple-compression risk and uplift-verification risk.

Fund-level economics

  • Management fee: 1.5–2.5% on committed/invested capital.
  • Carry: 20% above 8% LP preferred return; European waterfall in most modern funds.
  • For Long Lake's ~$1.28B raised: ~$20–30M/year management fee; potential GP carry $40M–$200M per fund cycle.
  • For GC Creation $1.5B: similar fee economics; venture-style vehicle so fund life is ~10 years.

06Go-to-Market — Sourcing and Exit

The seller is the founder-operator with no successor; sourcing runs through vertical trade associations and proprietary outreach; exit runs through PE-to-PE or strategic sale.

The seller persona

  • 55–70 years old, often founder.
  • Owns 50–100% of the equity.
  • $1M–$10M EBITDA business; $5M–$50M revenue.
  • No internal successor (~60% of SMB owners) or successor lacks capital.
  • Distrust of generic PE ("strip and flip" reputation).
  • Wants: liquidity, partial rollover equity to participate in upside, retention of team and brand, retention of family member roles, dignified exit timeline (often 2–5 years post-close).

Sourcing channels ranked by efficacy

  • 1. Vertical trade associations and conferences. NAHC (home care), CompTIA / ChannelCon (MSPs), AICPA (accounting), CIAB (insurance brokerages), CAI (community associations) — sponsor presence, named partners.
  • 2. Founder-friendly reputation / proprietary outreach. Shield's 60–90% structure with brand retention is itself a sourcing message. Long Lake's "we acquired your peer and kept their team" is the most powerful referral source.
  • 3. Vertical-specific M&A advisors and brokers. Mertz Taggart in home health, Cherry Bekaert in CPA, Marsh Berry in insurance, IT ExchangeNet in MSPs.
  • 4. Regional bankers and accountants. Lower-middle-market specialists with deal-flow visibility.
  • 5. IBBA-affiliated business brokers. Mostly main-street segment ($350K median sale price; BizBuySell Q3 2025), relevant at the Boomer transition margin.
  • 6. PE-to-PE transactions for platform-scale assets. Citrin Blackstone deal; Thoreau acquiring five existing PE-backed assets from New Mountain.

Deal cycle and pricing

LOI to close on $5M–$30M EBITDA target: 4–8 months typical; longer if regulatory (homecare CMS, brokerage state licensing transfer, CPA state-board approval). Diligence cost $300K–$1.5M. Earn-out structure on 40%+ of small business transactions per IBBA Market Pulse 2025; seller financing averages 30–40% of purchase price in main-street deals.

Typical purchase price mix: 60–75% cash at close (debt-financed), 10–25% rollover equity in the holdco or platform LLC, 10–20% earn-out tied to EBITDA retention or growth over 2–3 years.

Exit side — how the holdco gets sold

  • 1. Sponsor-to-sponsor recap. Citrin Cooperman sold by New Mountain to Blackstone in 2025 at ~15× EBITDA — proof transaction that PE-to-PE works in services.
  • 2. Strategic sale. Larger industry consolidator buys the platform (e.g., Hub International at $29B post-money in P&C brokerage).
  • 3. Take-public. Less common given holdco complexity; Amex GBT going private is the opposite direction.
  • 4. Continuation vehicle. GP-led secondary keeps the asset under management with new LP base.
  • 5. Asset-by-asset sale. Sell individual verticals separately if they have more strategic value to vertical-specific buyers.
Sources: IBBA Market Pulse Q2/Q3 2025; BizBuySell 2025 Year in Review; Insurance Journal on Hub International; International Accounting Bulletin on Citrin-Blackstone.
07Capital Structure and Founder Economics

Sponsor fund $50M–$1.5B; GP economics combine sponsor equity 5–15% plus 20% carry above 8% pref; hold periods 7–12 years per fund vintage.

Sponsor fund size to start

  • Minimum credible first fund: $50–200M.
  • Shield Tech: launched with $100M first close → $200M Feb 2026 add-on within 8 months.
  • Long Lake: reached $1.28B over ~2.5 years (founding 2023 → 2026); first close likely $200–400M.
  • Current (Crete): $500M for AI roll-up deployment.
  • GC Creation: $1.5B dedicated allocation.
  • Thoreau: $30B raised across Lexington / CPPIB / ICG / Middle East backers.

Debt-to-equity ratio

  • Typical LBO leverage 50–70% of EV at OpCo level.
  • Holdco-level PIK or preferred to stack more leverage on top in stable cash-flow verticals.
  • US LBO market hit record $397.5B in 2025 (Axios Pro Jan 2026), exceeding 2007 — credit markets supportive.
  • Caveat: Bain Global PE Report 2025/2026 highlights that easy multiple expansion via cheap debt has waned; "12 is the new 5" for required EBITDA growth.

Sponsor / principal economics

  • GP equity in the management company: typically 5–15% of holdco equity at funding.
  • GP carry: 20% on fund returns above 8% preferred return.
  • Typical successful fund GP carry payout: $50M–$200M for the GP team on a $500M–$1B fund returning 2–3× MOIC.
  • Founder dilution path: less aggressive than VC (no priced rounds during hold); main dilution from successor funds, hire-on of new partners, minority co-invest economics.

Hold periods and exit multiples

  • 7–12 years per fund vintage (standard PE).
  • Per OpCo: 3–7 years from acquisition to exit, often blended in a single platform sale.
  • Long Lake targets 3–5 year horizons to outperform the S&P 500 per Amex GBT press materials.
  • For the platform asset: 1.5–3× MOIC GP-level on successful funds.
  • For AI-services arbitrage specifically: PitchBook noted recent transactions exiting "well within the twos" for short-hold AI-services platforms — 2.x MOIC rather than 4× — because hold periods were short and AI uplift diligence limited.
Sources: Cambridge Associates fund terms 2024; Bain Global PE Report 2025/2026; Axios Pro US LBO volume 2026; PitchBook AI-services exit pricing.
08Risks and Failure Modes

Five recurring failure modes: buy-first-AI-later execution failure, unverifiable AI uplift, OpCo cultural resistance, fund concentration risk, vertical regulatory exposure.

  • 1. "Buy first, AI later" execution failure. Smarter Technologies (New Mountain) was formed May 2025 combining SmarterDx, Thoughtful.ai, and Access Healthcare into an AI-powered RCM platform. CEO Jeremy Delinsky stepped down December 2 via LinkedIn after 7 months; succeeded by President Michael Gao MD. Public framing: integration / leadership-fit difficulty under a tight time horizon. Even $1B+ raised and named AI-native portfolio companies cannot guarantee 12-month integration timelines.
  • 2. AI uplift cannot be independently verified at diligence. PitchBook's observation that recent AI-services exits priced "well within the twos" suggests buyers are discounting unverified margin claims. The structural risk: if uplift is real but unobservable to buyers, the platform exits at SMB multiples (5–7×) rather than platform multiples (10–14×) and the entire arbitrage collapses.
  • 3. Cultural and change-management resistance inside OpCos. MIT 2025 study: 95% of corporate AI initiatives fail to produce measurable returns; Gartner reports 85% of AI/ML projects fail; 42% of companies abandoned most AI initiatives in 2025; 75% of employees lack confidence using AI; 65% fear AI will eliminate their roles. When an OpCo's frontline operators resist deployment, the playbook stalls.
  • 4. Concentration risk in the holdco / fund. GC's Long Lake position is a substantial single-asset exposure per CNBC; if Long Lake's AI thesis fails, fund-level returns decline disproportionately. Thoreau's bet is concentrated in five healthcare-tech assets; if any one stumbles, $30B of valuation is sensitive. Concentration is structural — you cannot run a 50-position portfolio of 10× engineering-investment-per-asset.
  • 5. Regulatory exposure varies by vertical. Home care: CMS reimbursement, state licensing, labour-classification (W-2 vs 1099). MSP / cybersecurity: liability for client breaches; SEC cyber-disclosure rules. CPA: state-by-state ownership rules limiting non-CPA equity (the structural reason Crete uses an "alliance" model rather than direct equity). P&C brokerage: state licensing transfer in M&A; producer fiduciary obligations. Healthcare RCM: HIPAA, PHI handling, payor scrutiny.

Multiple-compression risk scenarios

  • Credit cycle reversal. A 200 bps rise in cost of debt compresses LBO equity returns ~30%. Bain 2025 notes the easy multiple expansion era is gone.
  • AI-uplift commodification. If every roll-up can deploy the same OpenAI-powered playbook (per Thrive/OpenAI's MSP and CPA work spreading), then AI uplift becomes the new baseline and no longer creates multiple arbitrage. Compression accelerates.

Preconditions for the playbook to work

  • SMB owner trust in the acquirer (sourcing).
  • Integration team capability (delivery).
  • Codified AI playbook with measurable KPIs (replication).
  • Debt market open at <8% all-in (capital structure).
  • LP appetite for the vehicle structure (fund management).
  • Vertical has labour-intensity that AI can address (margin lever).
Sources: Becker's Hospital Review, Modern Healthcare on Smarter Technologies CEO change; PitchBook on AI-services exits; MIT 2025 study; Gartner enterprise AI adoption data; Bain Global PE Report 2025/2026.
09When This Game Is Playable

The game requires M&A diligence muscle, operating integration capability, AI engineering depth, sustained LP capital — and a vertical where labour intensity is the margin lever.

Required team capabilities

  • M&A diligence and origination. Vertical-specific deal sourcing and QofE expertise; senior partners with bank/PE pedigree.
  • Operating integration. Post-close ops team that can install the playbook in 30–90 days; finance, HR, IT integration skill.
  • AI engineering. 5–15 ML/applied-AI engineers per vertical, with eval and reinforcement-learning experience.
  • LP relationship and capital formation. Sustained ability to raise $200M+ per fund vintage.
  • Vertical operating experience. Many holdcos hire ex-CEOs from the target vertical as platform CEOs.

Required capital base

  • $50–200M minimum first fund (Shield Tech's $100M floor is plausibly the lower bound).
  • $500M–$1.5B for a multi-vertical platform (Current $500M; Long Lake $1.28B; GC Creation $1.5B).
  • $20B+ for healthcare-tech-scale rollups (Thoreau $30B target).
  • Debt capacity at OpCo level: 50–60% of EV at $1M+ EBITDA targets; less for $200K–$1M micro-acquisitions.

Required market conditions

  • Fragmented vertical with willing sellers. Boomer succession crisis means supply of $1–10M EBITDA targets is high through 2030.
  • AI applicability proven in the vertical. Tax prep, HOA admin, MSP triage, RCM coding all have demonstrated AI productivity claims. Verticals with low AI applicability (high-touch physical services) are harder.
  • Debt market open. 2025 LBO volume at record $397.5B suggests open; Bain notes spread tightening.
  • LP appetite for AI-tagged buyout / hybrid vehicles. 40% of VC exits in 2025 are AI-tagged; LP rotation toward AI is structural through 2026.

Required time horizon

  • 7–12 years per fund vintage.
  • 3–7 years per OpCo hold.
  • 18–36 months from acquisition to first credible margin-uplift datapoint (long enough that fund-level patience is required).

Disqualifying conditions

  • Vertical already consolidated to top 3 players >60% market share — no acquisition runway.
  • Regulatory framework prohibiting non-licensee ownership (CPA, law firms in most states) — only structurally complex workarounds (the "alliance" model) available.
  • AI applicability unproven in vertical — labour cost is not the dominant cost and AI cannot meaningfully reduce it.
  • LP capital unwilling to underwrite >5-year payback periods.
  • Credit market spreads >800 bps over base — leveraged returns collapse.
  • Team lacks one of: vertical operating credibility, AI engineering depth, M&A origination muscle. Missing any one is fatal; missing two means the playbook cannot run.
