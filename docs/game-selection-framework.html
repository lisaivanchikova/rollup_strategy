Game Selection Framework

Working backwards from the founder cash-out at exit, this document maps the universe of business archetypes available on the US market when three enablers are present — domestic capital markets, SMB acquisition capability, and AI deployment capability — and scores each on the parameters that determine exit value.
PreparedJune 12, 2026
AuthorStrategy & Research
QuestionWhich game maximizes founder exit value
01Executive Summary

Three archetypes maximize absolute founder cash-out; a different three maximize probability of any exit at all. No single archetype dominates on both axes.

Bottom Line Up Front

Maximum theoretical founder cash-out is produced by software-economics games (horizontal AI platforms, vertical SaaS, AI agents-as-workforce). Maximum probability-weighted cash-out — adjusted for failure rates, capital intensity, and dilution — is produced by acquisition-plus-AI games (AI-native operating holdcos, data network compounders, vertical marketplaces). The two sets do not overlap. The choice is a preference declaration, not a ranking exercise.

Finding 01
Software multiples remain 3–5× higher than asset-heavy multiples.
Public vertical SaaS trades at 4–8× ARR. Frontier AI platforms clear 20–40× ARR in private rounds. Operating holdcos trade at 8–14× post-AI EBITDA. The multiple gap is the single largest determinant of exit value at the same revenue scale.
Finding 02
Founder ownership at exit varies by 8× across archetypes.
Bootstrapped software founders retain 40–60% at exit. VC-backed vertical SaaS founders retain 15–25%. Frontier AI founders often retain 4–10%. Ranking by absolute cash-out diverges sharply from ranking by enterprise value when dilution is included.
Finding 03
Only one archetype uses all three enablers at full structural strength.
Pure software archetypes leave the SMB enabler unused. Classic PE leaves the AI enabler underused. The intersection of US, SMB acquisition, and AI deployment at scores of 4 or 5 across all three is unique to the AI-Native Operating Holdco.
Sources synthesised from public exit data 2018–2026 (PitchBook, SEC filings, M&A advisory benchmarks); methodology in Appendix.
02The Question and Its Decomposition

Founder cash-out at exit decomposes into three multiplicative levers — multiple, scale, and retained ownership — each governed by the business archetype chosen.

Cash-out at exit is the cash a founder receives when the business is sold, taken public, or recapitalised, after dilution. It is the appropriate apex because it captures the practical objective of the entrepreneur and is the only number that exists with certainty after the fact. Three enablers are held constant: the US market, SMB acquisition capability, and AI deployment capability.

Maximize Founder Cash-Out at Exit │ ├── Lever 1 · Exit Multiple (what the buyer or market pays per unit of revenue or earnings) │ ├── Revenue predictability — recurring vs. project │ ├── Gross margin — software vs. services vs. assets │ ├── Growth sustainability — durable vs. cyclical │ ├── Defensibility — network, data, brand, regulation, switching cost │ └── Buyer universe — strategic, financial, public market, holdco │ ├── Lever 2 · Scale at Exit (the revenue or earnings on which the multiple is applied) │ ├── Market size — addressable demand │ ├── Distribution — owned, channel, viral, acquisition │ ├── Operating leverage — marginal cost of new revenue │ └── Capital recycling — reinvestment optionality │ └── Lever 3 · Retained Ownership (the share of enterprise value the founder receives) ├── Capital intensity — equity required to reach scale ├── Source of capital — bootstrap, VC, PE, debt, perpetual ├── Dilution per round — pricing and dilution mechanics └── Liquidation waterfall — preferences, ratchets, recapitalisation
Reading the tree

A high multiple with no scale produces a small business. Scale without retained ownership produces nothing for the founder. Ownership without multiple or scale produces a lifestyle business. All three levers must be optimised together; the archetype choice fixes the trade-off space. Software economics produce the highest multiples but typically require heavy dilution. Operating holdcos produce moderate multiples but retain higher founder ownership through sponsor equity plus carry. Services produce the lowest multiples regardless of dilution.

Decomposition adapted from standard enterprise value frameworks; ownership benchmarks from Carta and Cooley LP studies 2020–2025.

08Decision Framework

The lenses translate into a conditional rule: state the founder objective, and the archetype recommendation follows.

Not a ranking. A conditional mapping from founder objective to archetype, given the three enablers held constant. The rule is descriptive of the underlying economics, not of any particular operator's preference.

If the objective isMaximum theoretical absolute cash-out (top of distribution)
Then the archetype isHorizontal AI Platform for liquid outcome; Permanent Capital Compounder for paper outcome over decades.
If the objective isMost reliable $100M–$400M liquid cash-out (median outcome)
Then the archetype isAI-Native Operating Holdco, with Data Network Compounder as alternative when a unique data position can be assembled.
If the objective isAsymmetric edge against operators missing any one enabler
Then the archetype isAI-Native Operating Holdco; no other archetype uses all three enablers at structural strength.
If the objective isInter-generational wealth without liquidity event
Then the archetype isPermanent Capital Compounder; the only archetype designed for the no-exit objective.
If the objective isAvoiding the failure pattern in the universe
Then the archetype isNot Tech-Enabled Services Roll-Up; documented failure pattern across multiple attempts.
Composite reading

No archetype satisfies all objectives. The Horizontal AI Platform maximises the top of the cash-out distribution but offers 4–10% founder ownership at exit and leaves the SMB enabler idle. The AI-Native Operating Holdco offers the best median outcome and full enabler utilisation, but produces a ceiling of $400M–$1B rather than $1B–$5B. The Permanent Capital Compounder produces the largest paper wealth but no liquidity event. The choice rests on which trade-off the founder prefers.
