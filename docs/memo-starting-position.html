Maximizing the Starting Position — Comparative Analysis of Seven Day-Zero Levers for AI-Native Operating Holdco

The archetype is chosen — AI-Native Operating Holdco. This memo asks a separate question: across seven Day-Zero levers (vertical, LP base, operator structure, advisors, legal, capital, team and brand), which positions maximise the probability of a strong founder-profit outcome on a 4–7 year horizon? Each lever is evaluated as an open question. The vertical, the LP base, the operator structure — none are pre-decided. Recommendations are outputs of comparative analysis against documented reference operators, not validations of any plan already on paper.

01The Question and the Method

Given that the archetype is chosen, what Day-Zero positions across seven levers maximise the probability of strong founder profit on a 4–7 year horizon? Each lever is left open; conclusions emerge from comparative analysis.

Bottom Line Up Front

This memo does not assume the answers. For each of the seven levers — vertical, LP base, operator structure, advisors, legal, capital, team and brand — multiple options are scored on documented evidence from the four reference operators in the archetype (Long Lake, GC Creation, Shield Technology Partners, Crete / Current) and against the failure pattern (Smarter Technologies / NMC). The vertical analysis ranks five Hell-Yes candidates against a weighted scoring matrix and shows how the ranking shifts under different weight schemes. Each subsequent lever applies the same open structure. The recommended composite position is the output of the analysis; if the weights change, the position changes.

The frame Nicolas set

  • The archetype is chosen — AI-Native Operating Holdco per Game Selection Framework (June 12, 2026; score 100/125; only archetype using all three enablers at structural strength).
  • Target horizon for "good exit" — 4 to 7 years. Game Selection's parameter range for this archetype was 7–12 years; the 4–7 year target is the upper quartile of cycle speed.
  • Other archetypes remain options that can be played in the US; they are addressed in the Combinations Memo (June 14, 2026) and not re-litigated here.
  • With the game chosen, the question is the winning starting position via partners, legal structure, industry — and four other levers this memo adds because they are equally Day-Zero (LP base composition, capital structure, team composition, brand identity).

The capitalisation envelope from Game Selection — what we are optimising within

For Archetype 06 · AI-Native Operating Holdco the Game Selection Framework documented: exit multiple 8–14× EBITDA post-AI uplift; capital required $200–500M sponsor fund (the documented entry range, not a hard floor); founder ownership 20–35% blended via sponsor equity plus GP carry; realistic absolute founder cash-out $200M–$400M at probability-weighted median. This memo asks how to push the configuration to the upper quartile of that distribution — not how to change the envelope itself.

Method for each lever section (04–10)

  • Frame the open question — what is being decided, not what has been decided.
  • Present 3–5 documented options — each from a reference operator or established structural pattern.
  • Apply criteria with explicit weights — Multiple, Scale, Retained Ownership, Probability factors from the math in Section 02.
  • Score and rank — show the calculation, not just the conclusion.
  • Surface trade-offs — show what changes the ranking if weights shift.
  • Recommend with reasoning — the recommendation follows from the scoring; it is not the starting point.

02Reading the Math — Four Factors of Founder Profit

Founder profit decomposes into four multiplicative factors. Day-Zero levers move three; the fourth — probability of exit on horizon — is the survival rate that compounds against every other choice.

From the Game Selection Framework, founder cash-out at exit is a function of three levers — Exit Multiple × Scale × Retained Ownership. For the AI-Native Operating Holdco archetype operating on a 4–7 year horizon, those levers operate against a fourth — the probability that the exit clears at all on the target horizon. Day-Zero decisions move all four.

Founder Profit at Exit = Multiple × Scale × Retained Ownership × Probability of Exit on Horizon │ ├── Multiple — what the buyer or market pays per unit of EBITDA at exit │ ├── Determined by vertical multiple-arbitrage gap (Lever 1) │ ├── Determined by buyer universe at exit (Lever 4 advisor relationships) │ └── Determined by audit-quality EBITDA + verified AI uplift (Lever 7 engineering) │ ├── Scale — EBITDA on which the multiple is applied │ ├── Determined by capital deployable + reserve ratio (Lever 6) │ ├── Determined by acquisition cadence (Lever 4 advisors, Lever 7 team) │ └── Determined by vertical fragmentation supply (Lever 1) │ ├── Retained Ownership — share of EV the founder receives │ ├── Determined by GP commit + carry + HoldCo equity allocation (Lever 5) │ ├── Determined by Vintage 2 dilution path (Lever 2 LP terms) │ └── Determined by QSBS Section 1202 eligibility (Lever 5 — up to 100% federal exclusion on $10M per shareholder) │ └── Probability of Exit on Horizon — survival rate of the configuration ├── Determined by team integration discipline (Lever 7) ├── Determined by LP base patience (Lever 2) ├── Determined by vertical regulatory exposure (Lever 1) └── Determined by AI uplift verifiability (Lever 1 + Lever 7 — PitchBook documented AI-services exits at 2.x MOIC when uplift unverified)

Worked example — what "good exit" looks like in this archetype

Using the AI-Native Operating Holdco deep dive's $5M EBITDA MSP target as a transparent illustration (numbers from the deep dive, not invented): acquisition price $30M at 6×; debt $18M at 60%; equity $12M; AI deployment $1M one-time; 18 months later EBITDA grows to $6M (400 bps margin uplift on $25M revenue); exit at 11× platform multiple = $66M EV; after debt $18M repaid, equity proceeds $48M; 4× MOIC equity return at ~41% IRR on ~4-year hold. Replicate across 4–7 OpCos in a vertical → consolidated platform exit at $200–500M EV → founder share at 20–35% retained ownership → $40–175M per founder on the first vintage.

The reverse case is also documented. Smarter Technologies / NMC: $1B+ raised, integration failure within 7 months, CEO replaced via LinkedIn announcement (Becker's Hospital Review, Modern Healthcare, December 2025). PitchBook on recent AI-services exits "well within the twos" — operators with unverified AI uplift exit at 2× MOIC rather than 4×. These are the failure-mode endpoints the Day-Zero position is built to avoid.

What "winning" means in scoring

Throughout Sections 04–10, scoring uses a 1–5 scale where 5 represents an option strongest on the dimension at the documented reference-operator benchmark, and 1 represents the option weakest by the same standard. Weighted composite scores are calculated for each lever, with sensitivity to weight shifts shown in Section 11. No lever is decided on a single score; the composite picture is the output.

What the analysis tells us

  • HOA / Property Management is the most robust pick — wins on default and three of four sensitivity schemes; never falls below third. If the goal is maximising probability of strong founder profit on a 4–7 year horizon with documented reference-operator de-risking — HOA / Property Management is the recommended primary vertical, with the path Long Lake walked from 2023 to 2026 (HOA primary, adjacent residential and infrastructure services bolt-ons) as the most direct precedent. The second vertical activates only after the first cell clears a productivity gate.

If the goal is maximising absolute Multiple factor at the cost of higher unknown — P&C tier-2 is the answer, but the lack of direct reference operator at scale means the operator is in a "be the first" position rather than a "follow the playbook" position. This memo does not recommend that trade for a Vintage 1 emerging manager because the failure cost (Smarter Tech / NMC at $1B+ raised) is documented and material.

If the team holds specific deep expertise in any one vertical that this analysis does not weight — Accounting (with CPA alliance experience), HomeCare (with CMS expertise), or P&C (with state insurance background) — the weighting should be revised and the analysis re-run. The framework is the contribution; the score is the function of the inputs.

LPA provisions that any chosen LP archetype should support

  • GP commit — institutional standard 1–2% of committed capital.

Reading the analysis

  • Majority-with-rollover (B) is the modal AI-Holdco structure — used by Long Lake and Shield, the two highest-velocity reference operators in the archetype. Combines integration control with operator retention; capital efficiency at 60–75% means each $X of equity acquires $1.33–1.67X of EV; cross-OpCo platform integration enabled by holding majority stake.
  • 100%-acquired (A) requires permanent capital — Berkshire's model works because the seller has explicit confidence in indefinite-hold structure and management non-interference culture compounded over 60 years. For a fund-vintage Holdco on a 4–7 year horizon, 100%-acquired without rollover removes operator alignment — operator has cash, no remaining stake, no reason to drive AI uplift.
  • Alliance (C) is required where state-board rules block direct ownership — CPA vertical and law firms in most US states. Probability of integration is high but capital efficiency lower because no equity is acquired; HoldCo earns through alliance fees and revenue share. Acceptable when vertical mandates it (Crete precedent); avoid otherwise.
  • Minority growth equity (D) does not fit the AI-Holdco thesis — operator retains majority and the cross-OpCo platform integration is voluntary not structural; AI uplift cannot be enforced at the OpCo level.

Recommendation

Majority-with-rollover (B) recommended as standard. Specific structural elements documented across the reference operators:

  • Acquired stake 60–75% cash at close. Shield documented at 60–90%; Long Lake variable; the lower end of the range (60%) preserves operator alignment best, the upper end (75–90%) maximises integration control. For Vintage 1 risk profile, 60–70% is the typical entry.
  • Rollover equity 20–25% in HoldCo or platform LLC. Operator participates in upside via HoldCo equity; AI uplift becomes their gain too.
  • Earn-out 10–20% tied to two gates over 24–36 months: EBITDA retention/growth, and AI uplift KPI achievement (productivity, accuracy, margin target per vertical playbook). The two-gate earn-out aligns operator incentives with the AI thesis specifically — without this, operator can "coast" on legacy EBITDA without engaging AI deployment, and the multiple-arbitrage thesis fails at exit.
  • Sub-brand retention and operating CEO continuity for 24+ months post-close. Shield Tech publishes this as core sourcing message. Long Lake's "we acquired your peer and kept their team" is documented referral mechanic.

If the vertical selected mandates alliance (CPA), structure C is required and capital efficiency must be modelled accordingly — HoldCo earns through alliance fees and AI uplift revenue share, not equity gain. Reference: Crete / Current's $428M revenue under alliance structure.

08Lever 5 · Legal Structure — Three Architectural Decisions

Three architectural decisions: fund jurisdiction (Delaware LP standard), Holdco entity type (Delaware C-corp for QSBS), and OpCo structure (LLCs except where state regulation requires C-corp / alliance). LPA provisions enable Vintage 2 conversion optionality and minimise Section 206 friction.

LEVER 5 · OPEN QUESTION
What fund + Holdco + OpCo legal architecture maximises QSBS Section 1202 eligibility, minimises Section 206 friction, and preserves Vintage 2 evergreen conversion optionality?
Legal structure decisions are made at Day-Zero and are materially harder to reverse later. QSBS specifically requires C-corp issuance at HoldCo formation — restructuring later forfeits the tax treatment for new issuances. Section 206 risk is locked in by whether the AI Engineering arm operates as an internal department or a separate entity.

Architectural Decision 1 — Fund jurisdiction and structure

# Option Consequence
A Delaware Limited Partnership (LP) US LP standard; ERA-exempt SEC status if AUM under $150M; standard LPA templates; US-LP-friendly tax treatment for US LPs; recommended default for US-focused HoldCo
B Cayman Islands LP Offshore preferred by non-US LPs; tax-neutral feeder funds; introduces additional structuring cost ($30–60K legal); not aligned with US-only HoldCo thesis
C Delaware LLC fund Pass-through but loses LP structure familiarity; nonstandard for institutional LPs; not recommended

Delaware LP (A) recommended — US-focused HoldCo with US LP base benefits from standard structure; ERA exemption keeps regulatory overhead minimal at sub-$150M AUM.

Architectural Decision 2 — Holdco entity type and QSBS Section 1202 eligibility

# Option QSBS treatment Other considerations
A Delaware C-corp HoldCo, QSBS eligible at formation Up to 100% federal capital gains exclusion on the first $10M (or 10× basis, whichever greater) per shareholder per issuer Requires aggregate gross assets under $50M at issuance; 5+ year hold required; ≥80% of assets in qualified active trade
B Delaware LLC HoldCo QSBS not available — LLCs do not issue qualified small business stock Pass-through tax efficient for some structures; forfeits QSBS treatment for founders and Engineering team forever
C Out-of-state C-corp (Nevada, Wyoming) QSBS available if active trade requirement met Less familiar to institutional investors; not standard for institutional LP diligence

Delaware C-corp at formation (A) recommended. Quantitative impact: per shareholder, qualified gain up to $10M federally tax-free under Section 1202 100% exclusion (for stock issued after September 28, 2010). At the top long-term capital gains rate of 23.8% (20% LTCG + 3.8% NIIT), this saves up to $2.38M of federal tax per shareholder per issuer. Across 5 founder and key-engineer shareholders, $11.9M in federal tax savings preserved. The aggregate-gross-assets-under-$50M test is met at Holdco formation; later top-up funding above $50M does not affect QSBS treatment for stock already issued.

Architectural Decision 3 — OpCo structure and Section 206 posture

# Option Section 206 posture Consequence
A Wholly-owned OpCo LLCs, AI Engineering as internal Holdco department No related-party services arrangement; Section 206(3) does not arise Single P&L narrative; Pure HoldCo Decision Memo posture; cleanest LP story
B Wholly-owned OpCo LLCs, AI Engineering spun out as separate Delaware C-corp providing services to OpCos Section 206(3) related-party transaction risk on every portco service contract Documented enforcement: Apollo $52.7M (2016), TZP $500K+ (2025), Energy Capital and Insight Venture settled; per Pure HoldCo Decision Memo
C Alliance OpCos (where state regulation requires — CPA vertical) No equity ownership; alliance fees and revenue share; Section 206 inapplicable Required by state-board rules in CPA, law firms; capital efficiency model is different (no LBO equity gain)

Wholly-owned LLCs with AI Engineering as internal department (A) recommended. Aligns with the Pure HoldCo Decision Memo five-mechanism analysis; preserves single P&L narrative for LPs; eliminates documented Section 206 enforcement surface. Use alliance (C) only where state-board regulation requires it (Crete / CPA precedent).

LPA provisions that the legal architecture should support

  • Vintage 2 evergreen conversion option at LP level (Path B trigger per Combinations Memo) — LP-level option, not GP-forced.
  • Anchor LP ROFR for Vintage 2 at same fee economics.
  • Co-invest rights for anchor and strategic LPs (typically 1.5–2× pro-rata).
  • Key-person provision — fund deployment pauses if a defined fraction of founder GPs depart.
  • Carry waterfall — European (whole-fund); 8% pref; 20% carry; 100% catch-up; standard for emerging managers per Cambridge Associates 2024.
  • Management fee — 2% on committed during investment period; 1.5% on invested during harvest.
  • GP commit — 1–2% of committed capital; institutional standard.

09Lever 6 · Capital Structure — Three Allocation Philosophies

Three allocation philosophies span the documented spectrum: concentrated-platform (single large primary), balanced-diversified (split across primary + multiple bolt-ons), distributed-pilot (heavier paid pilot budget, smaller acquisitions). Balanced-diversified matches the modal AI-Holdco operator pattern and the documented failure-mode avoidance profile.

LEVER 6 · OPEN QUESTION
Across three allocation philosophies, which fits a 4–7 year exit horizon and at what LBO leverage level?
Fund-size envelope is not pre-fixed by this memo; the AI-Native Operating Holdco deep dive documents reference operator fund sizes from $100M (Shield first close) to $1.28B (Long Lake cumulative across vintages). The allocation philosophy operates within whatever Vintage 1 envelope is raised.

Three allocation philosophies

# Philosophy Allocation (% of fund) Pattern
A Concentrated-platform 50–60% primary platform / 15–20% bolt-on / 10–15% AI engineering / 5% paid pilots / 10% reserve Large primary platform anchors the vertical; bolt-ons are secondary. Higher concentration risk; mirrors GC Long Lake 85% concentration profile in extreme form. Suitable when primary platform target is unusually attractive.
B Balanced-diversified 25–35% primary platform / 25–35% bolt-ons / 12–18% AI engineering / 8–12% paid pilots / 12–18% reserve Diversification across 4–7 OpCos in single vertical; AI engineering fully funded; paid pilots support L2 of M&A funnel; operating reserve buffers shocks. Modal pattern across Shield, Crete, Long Lake bolt-on phases.
C Distributed-pilot 15–20% primary platform / 15–20% bolt-ons / 15–20% AI engineering / 25–30% paid pilots / 15–20% reserve Heavy paid pilot budget (L2 of M&A funnel) feeds large acquisition pipeline; smaller capital deployment per OpCo. Suitable when team prioritises sourcing optionality over deployment velocity. No documented reference operator follows this pattern at fund-vintage scale.

LBO leverage benchmarks

Per AI-Native Operating Holdco archetype deep dive: typical LBO leverage 50–70% of EV at OpCo level for stable cash-flow verticals; HOA/PM and P&C support upper range, MSP mid-range due to client-concentration risk, HomeCare lower due to CMS reimbursement caps. US LBO market hit record $397.5B in 2025 (Axios Pro January 2026) — credit markets supportive. Bain Global PE Report 2025/2026 caveat: easy multiple expansion via cheap debt has waned; "12 is the new 5" for required EBITDA growth.

  • Conservative 50–55% leverage — preserves OpCo flexibility for AI integration capex; reduces debt service risk during integration period; suitable for emerging-manager Vintage 1 risk profile.
  • Standard 60–65% leverage — mid-market PE norm; documented across reference operators.
  • Aggressive 70–80% leverage — squeezes deal economics tighter; raises debt service risk if integration is slow; suitable only with extremely stable OpCo cash flow profile.

Recommendation

Balanced-diversified allocation (B) at 25–35% / 25–35% / 12–18% / 8–12% / 12–18% structure, with OpCo-level LBO leverage at standard 60–65%. Reasoning:

  • Balanced-diversified maximises composite score across all criteria except absolute Scale (where concentrated-platform wins at the cost of probability).
  • Diversification across 4–7 OpCos in single vertical is the modal pattern across reference operators (Shield 9 MSPs, Long Lake 18–30 acquisitions, Crete ~30 CPA firms) — and the explicit counter-pattern to the GC Long Lake 85% concentration risk that PitchBook documents as the structural failure surface.
  • Standard 60–65% leverage balances debt service safety with capital efficiency at OpCo level.
  • Operating reserve at 12–18% buffers integration shocks; Berkshire's discipline of holding $397B in T-bills through 2024–2026 is the principle (not the absolute level) — do not deploy because capital is available; deploy because targets meet the diligence threshold.
Sources: AI-Native Operating Holdco archetype deep dive (Bain Global PE Report 2025/2026; Axios Pro US LBO volume 2026; Cambridge Associates fund waterfalls); Permanent Capital Compounder archetype deep dive (Berkshire T-bill discipline); Pure HoldCo Decision Memo (L2 paid pilot budget allocation).
10Lever 7 · Team and Brand — Composition Architecture and Identity Architecture

Team composition follows the documented mid-market PE Holdco pattern with one critical addition — a senior AI Engineering Lead with enterprise productisation background. Brand identity follows the documented reference operator pattern of distinct entity branding (Long Lake, Shield Technology Partners, Crete / Current) over founder personal branding.

LEVER 7 · OPEN QUESTION
What GP team size, AI Engineering Lead profile, vertical leadership model, and brand identity architecture maximise LP credibility, seller trust, and AI talent recruiting?
Team composition determines Probability via integration discipline (Smarter Tech / NMC failure case is leadership and integration, not capability). Brand determines Scale via inbound sourcing (the "we acquired your peer and kept their team" Long Lake mechanic depends on a recognisable name).

C 3+ GP partners Larger team; vertical specialist + AI specialist + finance specialist mix; higher overhead but broader capability coverage; common at $200M+ vintages; less common at Vintage 1 emerging-manager scale

Vertical leadership model options

# Model Pattern
A Vertical Lead per active vertical (operator-CEO background) Recruited from $20–80M revenue OpCo CEO pool in the chosen vertical; runs pilot delivery and post-acquisition deployment; seller credibility; AI uplift KPI execution. Matches Shield Tech and Crete patterns.
B Single cross-vertical operations lead Adequate for single-vertical Vintage 1; does not scale to multi-vertical. Lower cost; lower seller credibility per vertical.
C In-team coverage by GPs (no dedicated Vertical Lead) Documented failure mode if GP capacity proves insufficient; matches the Smarter Tech / NMC "buy first, AI later" pattern when integration leadership is shallow.

Brand identity options

# Identity architecture Pattern and reference evidence
A Distinct entity brand Documented across all four reference operators: Long Lake Management Holdings, Shield Technology Partners, GC Creation, Crete / Current. Brand independent of founder personal identity; signals institutional seriousness to LPs and sellers; supports referral mechanic ("we acquired your peer and kept their team").
B Founder personal name brand Used by smaller search funds and family-office vehicles; not documented at AI-Holdco scale; constrains team scaling because brand depends on founder presence.
C Co-branded with strategic LP Used in Path D LP archetype (Lever 2); Crete benefits indirectly from Thrive / OpenAI co-branding; constrains independence and exit narrative; suitable only when strategic LP relationship is structural.

Recommendation

  • GP team: 2 partners (B) — documented modal pattern for mid-market PE Holdco; redundancy without overhead.
  • AI Engineering Lead: enterprise productisation profile (B) — matches the talent pool reference operators recruit from; production system discipline is the documented requirement.
  • Vertical Lead per active vertical from operator-CEO pool (A) — matches Shield and Crete patterns; the cost is recruiting overhead, the benefit is seller credibility and AI uplift KPI execution.
  • Distinct entity brand (A) — documented across all four reference operators; supports referral mechanic and Vintage 2 LP credibility; preserve research-arm public artefacts (rollup-economy.com framework content) under the entity brand as the research arm.
  • QSBS-eligible HoldCo equity as part of AI Lead and Vertical Lead compensation — Carta and Sentiro Partners 2026 benchmarks document $260K+ base + $50K equity for Vertical Lead and $300K+ base for AI Engineering Lead; HoldCo equity grant in the 1–3% pool with QSBS Section 1202 eligibility on first $10M of qualified gain.
