Methodology01Inclusion criteria

Three hard tests separate true AI-integrator roll-ups from adjacent noise: buys operators, integrates AI inside them, and runs a HoldCo (or three-entity) structure.

A prior draft of this landscape listed 43 companies. The audit re-applied three filters from the AI-Native Operating HoldCo definition. Only seven survived all three. The other 36 are retained as context in four appendix buckets — Disruption Watchlist, Capital Ecosystem, Structural References, Adjacent Ecosystem — each labelled with the explicit reason it is not a competitor.

The three filters

F1 · Buys operatorsAcquires going-concern SMB operating businesses in a vertical and owns them on balance sheet.Vertical AI SaaS vendors that sell software to regulated industries (Harvey, Sierra, Decagon, Hippocratic, Glean, Hebbia, Cresta, Eudia-as-product).
F2 · AI insideEmbeds proprietary AI tooling inside each portfolio company to drive productivity uplift in the existing workflow.AI consulting / FDE shops that deliver AI to third-party customers (Distyl, BCG X, McKinsey QB, Accenture Song, OpenAI DeployCo).
F3 · HoldCo structureEither explicit three-entity Fund + AI-Integration Co + HoldCo, or a single operating HoldCo with an AI thesis.Classical software holdcos without an integrated AI thesis (Constellation, Banyan, Roper, Permanent Equity, CODI).

Why the demotions matter

  • Distyl AI — earlier rankings placed Distyl as a Tier-1 competitor. It is AI consulting / services for Fortune 500 and does not acquire operators. Risk that its sponsors spin up an adjacent acquisition vehicle within 12–18 months is tracked on the Watchlist; today it is not a direct competitor.
  • Trilogy / ESW (Joe Liemandt) — classical software PE with wage-arbitrage delivery and AI overlay. Buys B2B software, not SMB operators. Structural reference for the perpetual-capital model, not a roll-up of operators.
  • Eudia, Accrual, Beacon Software — vertical AI SaaS sold to regulated industries, not roll-ups of them. Disruption signal, not direct competitor.

Archetype validators05Long Lake and Smarter Technologies

Long Lake validates the AI-Native HoldCo mechanic at $6.3B exit scale. Smarter Technologies / NMC validates the failure mode of the inverse cadence. Both operate in verticals out of scope for the support-process thesis but inform mechanism design.

Long Lake Management Holdings — positive validator

$1.28B raised across the NewCo. Stayed primarily in HOA and adjacent residential property management for the first ~18 acquisitions. Internal AI platform (Nexus) is approximately 80% horizontal and 20% vertical, model-agnostic. Documented uplift: 25–30% per-FTE productivity, integration cadence compressed from years to days.

Killer data point: May 2026 agreement to take Amex Global Business Travel private at $6.3B EV — 12× EBITDA, 60.2% premium to public price. Proves the AI-rollup HoldCo mechanic can scale to public-company acquisition size. Reported in Skift, Reuters, BusinessWire.

Smarter Technologies / NMC — failure-mode case

May 2025 three-way combination of SmarterDx (clinical AI), Access Healthcare (RCM operations), and Thoughtful.ai (RPA / agents). Effectively a multi-asset combination of pre-existing AI tools without integration discipline. CEO out at month 7 — reported in Becker's Hospital Review and Modern Healthcare (December 2025).

What the case demonstrates: the inverse cadence — acquire the portfolio first and deploy AI afterward — without an explicit productivity gate at acquisition produces measurable execution risk inside the first year. A negative reference for any HoldCo design that relies on post-acquisition AI deployment to justify multiple arbitrage.

Patterns06Five structural patterns

Five structural patterns appear across the Tier-1 operators. Each pattern is a design choice in the AI-Native HoldCo archetype; each has visible variants across the operator set.

Pattern 1 — Permanent capital as structural moat

Thrive Holdings and Trilogy/ESW (structural reference) and Long Lake all use perpetual-capital structures. GC Creation has publicly discussed adding a permanent vehicle in Fund XIII. The reasoning: 10-year LP fund mechanics force-sell HoldCos at year 8, compressing compound value before the AI uplift fully realises.

Pattern 2 — Talent concentration in a single network

The "Palantir mafia" recurs as the talent pool for AI Engineering Lead and Forward-Deployed Engineer (FDE) roles across multiple operators. Pedigree pattern at AI lead roles: 8–13 years of Palantir Forward-Deployed Engineering tenure, often a former Palantir CIO or product lead, sometimes a Palantir BD lead who built Foundry commercial.

Pattern 3 — Frontier-model partnerships, not generic API consumption

OpenAI took equity in Thrive Capital in December 2025, embedding researchers and engineers inside Crete and Shield. Distyl runs the OpenAI Services Alliance since 2023. GC Creation uses Mistral as a default model across portcos. Each operator has a primary frontier-model relationship; Anthropic enterprise and xAI lanes remain open across the set.

Pattern 4 — 60–90% acquisition with seller rollover is standard

Shield acquires 60–90% with 10–40% rollover. Long Lake universally retains founders / CEOs. Current/Crete retains partner-firm brand and local autonomy. Marc Bhargava (GC) has publicly framed 30% seller rollover as "the key differentiator versus traditional PE." Standard earn-out window across the set: 12–24 months.

Pattern 5 — Captive AI tooling, not licensed SaaS

Sentinel and Spectre (Shield) are platform-owned, not available to non-Shield MSPs. Distillery and Context Mesh (Distyl) are Palantir Foundry Ontology analogues, owned. Nexus AI (Long Lake) is ~80% horizontal / 20% vertical, model-agnostic. The pattern across the set: own the tooling layer, lease the models.

Pattern 6 — Vertical specialisation beats horizontal scale in early platform years

Shield's strongest sub-segments are vertical-specialised: dental practices, energy-sector IT, biotech / financial-services IT. Long Lake is HOA-only. Current/Crete is accounting-only. Joe Liemandt's Trilogy / ESW (75–100 acquisitions across software) is the exception, not the rule. Vertical-niche beats horizontal scale in years 1–3.

Failure modes07Thrasio and OpenStore — five lessons for HoldCo design

Thrasio raised $3B+ and filed Chapter 11 in February 2024 at a $10B peak valuation. OpenStore went from $970M to ~$50M. Both Amazon FBA aggregators. Five lessons translate directly to any AI-integrator HoldCo design.

  1. Deferred-payment acquisitions create earnings-recursion risk. Thrasio paid sellers in 2–3 year tranches contingent on portco performance. When pandemic-era e-commerce demand normalised, the tranches could not be funded. Upfront cash plus earn-out on organic-growth metrics is the safer structure.
  2. No operational moat means no roll-up. Amazon FBA brands had no defensible technology layer. Each brand was independently buyable. Vertical AI built inside the operator is the moat that makes the HoldCo defensible.
  3. Capital availability is not thesis validation. 2020–21 easy money funded the aggregator boom; subsequent rate hikes killed it. Any HoldCo design needs to clear a 5%+ Fed-funds environment from formation.
  4. Multiple compression works only with operational uplift. Thrasio paid 4–5× EBITDA and got nothing structural in return. Sub-$1M EBITDA insurance-brokerage arbitrage 5× → 11–16× requires AI margin uplift backing the multiple expansion, not financial engineering alone.
  5. Single-platform concentration is a single point of failure. Diversification across multiple support-process verticals — even a small number — is structurally safer than concentration in one operator type.
